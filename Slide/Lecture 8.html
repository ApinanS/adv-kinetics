Lecture 8-1

What is the heat of reaction, and how does it change with temperature?

Definition

Heat of reaction from heats of formation

$$\Delta H_{Rx}^{\circ}(T_R)=\sum_i \nu_i\, \Delta H_{f,i}^{\circ}(T_R)\qquad T_R = 25^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$$
$\Delta H_{Rx}<0$exothermic — releases heat, raises reactor T
$\Delta H_{Rx}>0$endothermic — absorbs heat, lowers reactor T
Basis = 1 mole of the limiting reactant (usually its stoichiometric coefficient is 1).

The building block

Standard enthalpy of formation $\Delta H_f^{\circ}$

Definition

Heat to form 1 mol of a compound from its elements at the reference state, $25^{\circ}$C and 1 bar.

Phase specified

Tabulated for the species' phase — gas, liquid or solid — at that reference state.

Elements = 0

Pure elements ($\mathrm{O_2}$, $\mathrm{H_2}$, C…) have $\Delta H_f^{\circ}=0$ by definition.

Temperature correction

Enthalpy at the reaction temperature

$$\Delta H_{Rx}(T)=\Delta H_{Rx}^{\circ}(T_R)+\underbrace{\int_{T_R}^{T}\Delta C_p\,dT}_{\text{sensible-heat correction}}$$
$$\Delta C_p=\sum_i \nu_i\,C_{p,i}\qquad C_{p,i}=a_i+b_iT+c_iT^2+d_iT^3$$

If $\Delta C_p$ is small (or $C_p$ taken constant), $\Delta H_{Rx}(T)\approx\Delta H_{Rx}^{\circ}$ — a common simplification.

Getting to the reaction state

Account for phase changes on the way

$$H_i(T)=\Delta H_{f,i}^{\circ}+\!\int_{T_R}^{T_b}\!C_p^{L}\,dT+\Delta H_{vap}+\!\int_{T_b}^{T}\!C_p^{G}\,dT$$
If a species is fed as liquid but reacts as vapour, add the latent heat $\Delta H_{vap}$ at its boiling point $T_b$.
Liquid → Liquidsensible heat only
Liquid → Gassensible $+\;\Delta H_{vap}$
Gas → Gassensible heat only

What is the energy balance of a reactor, and how is it linked to the heat of reaction?

First law on an open system

General reactor energy balance

$$\dot Q-\dot W_s+\sum_i F_{i0}H_{i0}-\sum_i F_iH_i=\frac{dE_{sys}}{dt}$$

$\dot Q$ = heat added, $\dot W_s$ = shaft work done by the system, $H_i$ = molar enthalpy of species $i$.

Substituting $H_i=H_i^{\circ}+\!\int C_{p,i}dT$ turns the enthalpy sums into a heat-of-reaction term plus a sensible-heat term.

Where the working equation comes from

From the first law to the reactor form

$\dot Q-\dot W_s+\sum F_{i0}H_{i0}-\sum F_iH_i=0$steady-state first law
$H_i=H_i^{\circ}(T_R)+\int_{T_R}^{T}C_{p,i}\,dT$split: formation + sensible heat
$\sum(F_{i0}-F_i)H_i^{\circ}=-\sum_j r_jV\Delta H_{Rxj}$the reaction-heat term emerges
$\dot Q-\dot W_s-\sum_j r_jV\Delta H_{Rxj}=\sum F_{i0}C_{p,i}(T-T_0)$the form we solve

The form we actually solve

Steady-state working equation

$$\dot Q-\dot W_s-\sum_j r_j V\,\Delta H_{Rxj}=\sum_i F_{i0}\,C_{p,i}\,(T-T_0)$$
heat generated by reaction $-\,r_jV\Delta H_{Rxj}$ sensible heat to raise feed to $T$
Unsteady (batch/CSTR start-up) adds $\big(\sum_i N_iC_{p,i}\big)\dfrac{dT}{dt}$ on the right.
Set $\dot Q=\dot W_s=0$ (adiabatic): every joule of reaction heat feeds $\sum F_{i0}C_{p,i}(T-T_0)$, so $T$ climbs until the reactants — and the heat release — run out.

Interactive — the energy-balance line

Adiabatic temperature rise: $T=T_0+\dfrac{(-\Delta H_{Rx})X}{\sum \Theta_iC_{p,i}}$

More exothermic or lower $\sum\Theta C_p$ → steeper line → hotter exit.

Two source terms in stirred tanks

Heat duty $\dot Q$ and shaft work $\dot W_s$

Heat duty (jacket)

$\dot Q=UA\,\Delta T_{LM}$

$U$ = overall HT coeff., $A$ = jacket area, $\Delta T_{LM}$ = log-mean temperature difference with the coolant.

Shaft work (impeller)

$\dot W_s=N_p\,\rho\,N^3D^5$

Power number $N_p$, rotational speed $N$ (rps), impeller diameter $D$. Turbulent $Re>10^4$.

Sign rule

$\dot Q>0$ heat in
$\dot W_s>0$ work out

A stirrer adds energy, so it enters as $-\dot W_s$ (system receives work).

How to design a non-isothermal, steady-state mixed-flow reactor (CSTR)?

Two coupled balances

CSTR mole & energy balance

Mole balance (each species)

$$0=F_{i0}-F_i+\Big(\textstyle\sum_j \nu_{ij}r_j\Big)V$$

Energy balance (steady state)

$$\dot Q+\dot W_s-\!\sum_j r_jV\Delta H_{Rxj}=\!\sum_i F_{i0}C_{p,i}(T-T_0)$$
The two balances are coupled through $T$: rate constants $k_j(T)$ live in the mole balance, and the reaction heat lives in the energy balance.
Result: a set of nonlinear algebraic equations — perfect for EQUATRAN's simultaneous solver.

Removing the reaction heat

Jacket heat duty & log-mean ΔT

$$\dot Q=UA\,\Delta T_{LM},\quad \Delta T_{LM}=\frac{(T_{c,in}-T)-(T_{c,out}-T)}{\ln\!\dfrac{T_{c,in}-T}{T_{c,out}-T}}$$
The coolant balance $\dot Q=\dot m_{CW}C_p(T_{c,in}-T_{c,out})$ then fixes the cooling-water flow rate $\dot m_{CW}$.

The stirrer adds energy too

Impeller shaft work (power)

$$\dot W_s=N_p\,\rho\,N^3D^5\qquad Re=\frac{\rho N D^2}{\mu}$$
In turbulent flow ($Re>10^4$) the power number $N_p$ is constant — a fixed property of the impeller geometry.
Impeller type$N_p$ (turbulent)
4×45° pitched blade1.27
Rushton (6 flat blades)≈ 5.0
Marine propeller≈ 0.35

How to design a non-isothermal, steady-state plug-flow reactor (PFR)?

Now the balances are differential in volume

PFR mole & energy balance

Mole balance

$$\frac{dF_i}{dV}=\sum_j \nu_{ij}\,r_j$$

Energy balance

$$\Big(\textstyle\sum_i F_iC_{p,i}\Big)\frac{dT}{dV}=-\sum_j r_j\Delta H_{Rxj}+\dot Q_a$$
Along the tube each slice exchanges no mass with its neighbours — it behaves like a tiny batch moving downstream.
Gas phase$v$ changes with $T$, $P$, total moles
Liquid phase$v\approx v_0$ (constant density)

Concentration depends on the phase

Gas- vs liquid-phase concentrations

Liquid phaseGas phase
Volumetric flow$v\approx v_0$$v=v_0\dfrac{P_0}{P}\dfrac{T}{T_0}\dfrac{F_T}{F_{T0}}$
Concentration$C_i=F_i/v_0$$C_i=F_i/v$
As $T$ riseslittle changegas expands → $C_i$ falls
Gas expansion is a built-in brake on runaway: hotter gas is more dilute, so the rate partly self-limits — which is why the gas PFR peaks at a milder temperature than the liquid CSTR.

Wall heat exchange

The distributed cooling term $\dot Q_a$

$$\dot Q_a=Ua\,(T_a-T)\qquad Ua=\frac{4U}{D}$$

$a$ = heat-transfer area per unit reactor volume (m⁻¹); for a tube $a=4/D$.

$\dot Q_a>0$ heats the reactor ($T_a>T$); $\dot Q_a<0$ cools it. Set $Ua=0$ for adiabatic.

Differential energy balance

Energy balance on a tube slice

heat generated + heat exchanged = enthalpy changeslice of volume $dV$
$\dot Q_a\,dV-\sum_j r_j\Delta H_{Rxj}\,dV=\Big(\sum_i F_iC_{p,i}\Big)dT$balance across the slice
$\dfrac{dT}{dV}=\dfrac{-\sum_j r_j\Delta H_{Rxj}+Ua(T_a-T)}{\sum_i F_iC_{p,i}}$the ODE we integrate down the tube

How to design a non-isothermal, steady-state fixed-bed (packed) reactor?

Catalyst weight is the independent variable

Fixed-bed mole balance & bed properties

$$\frac{dF_i}{dW}=\sum_j \nu_{ij}\,r'_j$$
$W=(1-\phi)\rho_c A_c z$bed weight from length $z$
$\rho_b=(1-\phi)\rho_c$bulk density; $\phi$ = void
$r'_j$ is the rate per unit catalyst mass — the natural basis for a solid-catalysed reaction.
Void fraction $\phi$ and particle density set how much active catalyst a given bed volume holds.

Describing the packed bed

Void fraction & densities

$\phi=\dfrac{V_{void}}{V_{bed}}$void (empty) fraction
$\rho_b=(1-\phi)\rho_c$bulk (bed) density
$W=\rho_b\,A_c\,z$catalyst weight from bed length $z$

Dense particle

Non-porous — all mass is solid, so higher particle density $\rho_c$.

Porous particle

Internal pores host most of the catalytic area but lower $\rho_c$.

Pressure drop matters for gas beds

Ergun equation

$$\frac{dP}{dz}=-\frac{G}{\rho\,d_p}\Big(\frac{1-\phi}{\phi^3}\Big)\Big[\frac{150(1-\phi)\mu}{d_p}+1.75\,G\Big]$$

Pressure falls along the bed → concentrations (and rate) drop for a gas reaction, so $P$ must be solved together with $F_i$ and $T$.

$G=\rho u$ is the mass flux; friction factor $f$ groups the bracket in the code.

How to design a non-isothermal, steady-state reversible reactor?

Reversible reactions have a ceiling

Equilibrium conversion & design guideline

The ceiling

$X$ cannot pass $X_e(T)$, set by thermodynamics: $K_e=\prod C_i^{\nu_i}$.

Design target

Aim for $X=0.96\text{–}0.98\,X_e$ — chasing the last few % costs huge volume.

Shift it

Remove a product, or stage reactors with inter-stage heat exchange.

The rate vanishes at equilibrium

How close is close enough?

$$-r_A=k_1\Big(C_A^{a}C_B^{b}-\frac{C_C^{c}C_D^{d}}{K_e}\Big)$$
As $X\to X_e$ the bracket $\to 0$, so the rate $\to 0$. The last few percent of conversion demand a disproportionately large reactor.
Design rule: stop at $X=0.96$–$0.98\,X_e$. To go further, remove a product or add another cooled/heated stage.

Van 't Hoff behaviour

How $K_e$ moves with temperature

$$K_e=\frac{k_1}{k_2}\propto \exp\!\Big[\tfrac{\Delta H_{Rx}}{R}\big(\tfrac1{T_R}-\tfrac1T\big)\Big]$$
Exothermic$K_e\!\downarrow$ as $T\!\uparrow$ → $X_e$ falls
Endothermic$K_e\!\uparrow$ as $T\!\uparrow$ → $X_e$ rises
Exothermic reversible reactions face a trade-off: high $T$ gives fast rate but low attainable conversion. The sweet spot is a moderate temperature — or staged cooling.

Interactive — the equilibrium curve

Beating the exothermic trade-off

Adiabatic beds with inter-stage cooling

  • Each navy up-step = one adiabatic bed heating up as it converts.
  • Each blue dashed step-back = cooling between beds, resetting toward high $X_e$.
  • Staging climbs the staircase to high overall conversion the equilibrium would otherwise forbid.

The mirror image

Endothermic reactions: inter-stage heating

  • Endothermic $K_e$ rises with $T$ → high $T$ gives high $X_e$.
  • But each adiabatic bed cools as it converts, slowing the rate.
  • Re-heat (red steps) between beds to climb back toward high conversion.

Key Takeaways

1

Always solve the mole and energy balances together — $T$ couples them through $k(T)$ and $\Delta H_{Rx}$.

2

The reactor type sets the math: CSTR → algebraic; PFR / fixed-bed → ODE in $V$ or $W$ (add Ergun for gas beds).

3

Exothermic reactions need a heat-removal plan; adiabatic operation invites thermal runaway.

4

For reversible exothermic reactions, chase the optimum temperature or stage beds with inter-cooling.

5

In EQUATRAN, one skeleton — balances + rate law + integral … by rkv — solves every case.
