Lecture 2-1

What is the rate expression for a reaction controlled by an elementary single reaction?

Definition

What is an elementary reaction?

Single step

The reaction happens in one step — reactants convert to products directly, with no detectable intermediate.

Collision-driven

The rate is set by the collision of reactant molecules in the system (2 or 3 reactants meeting).

Order = stoichiometry

Because there is one step, the reaction order equals the molecularity read from the balanced equation.

Homogeneous, non-catalytic: everything happens in a single fluid phase.

Before we write an equation

What does the reaction rate depend on?

✓ Reactant concentration

More reactant → more collisions → faster rate. This is the heart of the rate law.

✓ Temperature

Sets the fraction of collisions energetic enough to react (Arrhenius).

✗ Stirrer speed

For a true homogeneous reaction, mixing does not change the intrinsic rate.

✗ Product concentration / pressure*

Not in the forward elementary rate law (*pressure only via concentration for gases).

Elementary reaction · Lecture 2-1

The rate law is a power law of concentrations

$$a\mathrm{A} + b\mathrm{B} \rightarrow c\mathrm{C} + d\mathrm{D} \qquad r_1 = k_r\, C_A^{\,a}\, C_B^{\,b}$$
$k_r$ = reaction rate constanttemperature-dependent (next slide)
$a + b$ = overall order of reactionfor elementary steps, $a,b$ = stoichiometric coefficients
$[\,r_1\,] = \dfrac{\text{mol}}{\text{m}^3\cdot\text{s}}$rate is intensive — per unit volume

Temperature dependence

Arrhenius equation — how $T$ enters the rate

$$k(T) = A\, e^{-E_a/RT} \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad k(T) = k(T_0)\, e^{\frac{E_a}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_0}-\frac{1}{T}\right)}$$
$A$ = frequency (pre-exponential) factorcollision frequency & orientation
$E_a$ = activation energy (kJ/mol)energy barrier the collision must clear
$R$ = gas constant, $8.314$ J/(mol·K)two-point form avoids needing $A$

Why $\ln k$ vs $1/T$ is a straight line

Take logs: $\ln k = \ln A - \dfrac{E_a}{R}\cdot\dfrac{1}{T}$  — slope $=-E_a/R$, intercept $=\ln A$.
slope $=-E_a/R=$ K  ·  a steeper slope ⇒ larger $E_a$

Lecture 2-2

How do we obtain the rate expression for a non-elementary reaction?

Definition

A non-elementary reaction is a sum of hidden steps

Apparent reaction

The overall equation is really many elementary sub-steps added together — the visible reaction is only the net result.

Chemical path

Adsorption, surface reaction, desorption — the molecular events that consume and form species.

Physical path

External & internal mass transfer, heat and momentum transfer that feed the chemical steps.

What the rate law looks like

Do not expect a clean power law

The measured rate is the sum of the rates of many sub-steps combined.
The form need not be a power law — it is often a ratio of concentration terms.
Even if it looks like a power law, the exponent (order) need not be an integer — e.g. order $\tfrac{3}{2}$ appears in Lecture 2-3.

Why we bother

Target: a rate in measurable variables

Write the rate using quantities we can actually measure — usually the limiting reactant or the primary product concentration.
$$\text{Fluid phase: } r = f(T,\,C_{\text{limiting}}) \qquad \text{Solid catalytic: } r = f(T,\,C_{\text{limiting, bulk}})$$
The expression then feeds reactor design through the mass & energy balances.

The workflow

How to determine a rate expression

Step 2, in detail

Two tools to collapse the mechanism

1 · Rate-limiting step (RLS)

The slowest step controls the overall rate. Any non-limiting reversible step is taken to be at equilibrium ($r=0$).

2 · Pseudo-steady-state (PSSH)

Reactive intermediates — radicals, surface / enzyme complexes — are assumed to have net rate $\approx 0$: they form and vanish as fast as they appear.

Both tools remove the unmeasurable intermediates, leaving a rate in measurable concentrations.

Lecture 2-3

Rate expression from multiple elementary steps — a chain reaction

Worked system · pyrolysis of acetaldehyde

An apparent reaction hides four elementary steps

$$\mathrm{CH_3CHO} \xrightarrow{\;k_{\text{eff}}\;} \mathrm{CH_4 + CO}$$
StepElementary reactionRate const.
Initiation$\mathrm{CH_3CHO} \xrightarrow{\;k_a\;} \mathrm{CH_3^{\bullet} + CHO^{\bullet}}$$k_a$
Propagation (RLS)$\mathrm{CH_3CHO + CH_3^{\bullet}} \xrightarrow{\;k_b\;} \mathrm{CH_4 + CH_3CO^{\bullet}}$$k_b$
Propagation$\mathrm{CH_3CO^{\bullet}} \;\underset{k_c'}{\overset{k_c}{\rightleftharpoons}}\; \mathrm{CH_3^{\bullet} + CO}$$k_c,\,k_c'$
Termination$\mathrm{2\,CH_3^{\bullet}} \xrightarrow{\;k_d\;} \mathrm{C_2H_6}$$k_d$

Reversible steps use $\rightleftharpoons$; a reversible non-limiting step is taken at equilibrium ($r=0$), while the irreversible RLS ($\rightarrow$) controls the overall rate.

Applying PSSH to the radicals

Sum the reaction rates of each radical to zero

Radicals $\mathrm{CH_3^{\bullet}},\ \mathrm{CHO^{\bullet}},\ \mathrm{CH_3CO^{\bullet}}$ are intermediates ⇒ each net rate $=0$.  Let $C_A \equiv C_{\mathrm{CH_3CHO}}$.
$$r_{\mathrm{CH_3^{\bullet}}} = \underbrace{k_a C_A}_{\text{initiation}} - \underbrace{k_b C_A C_{\mathrm{CH_3^{\bullet}}}}_{\text{step b}} + \underbrace{k_c C_{\mathrm{CH_3CO^{\bullet}}}}_{\text{step c}} - \underbrace{2k_d C_{\mathrm{CH_3^{\bullet}}}^{2}}_{\text{termination}} = 0$$
Propagation terms (b, c) cancel  ⇒  $k_a C_A = 2k_d C_{\mathrm{CH_3^{\bullet}}}^{2}$initiation balances termination
$\displaystyle C_{\mathrm{CH_3^{\bullet}}} = \left(\tfrac{k_a}{2k_d}\,C_A\right)^{1/2}$solve for the radical concentration

The result · Lecture 2-3

Substitute the radical back into the RLS

From the RLS (step b):  $-r_{\mathrm{CH_3CHO}} = k_b\,C_A\,C_{\mathrm{CH_3^{\bullet}}}$rate set by the slow step
Insert $C_{\mathrm{CH_3^{\bullet}}} = \left(\tfrac{k_a}{2k_d}\,C_A\right)^{1/2}$ from the previous slideeliminate the unmeasurable radical
$$-r_{\mathrm{CH_3CHO}} = k_b\left(\frac{k_a}{2k_d}\right)^{1/2} C_{\mathrm{CH_3CHO}}^{3/2} \;=\; k_{\text{eff}}\, C_{\mathrm{CH_3CHO}}^{3/2}$$
Overall order $\tfrac{3}{2}$ — non-integer — even though every hidden step is elementary.

What is the rate expression for an equilibrium (reversible) reaction?

Worked system · glycerol carbonate synthesis

Net rate = forward − reverse

$$\text{DMC} + \text{Glycerol} \underset{k'_{-1}}{\overset{k'_{1}}{\rightleftharpoons}} \text{Glycerol carbonate} + 2\,\text{CH}_3\text{OH}$$
$$r'_1 = k'_1 C_D C_{Gly} - k'_{-1} C_{GC} C_{Me} = k'_1\!\left(C_D C_{Gly} - \frac{C_{GC} C_{Me}}{K_e}\right)$$

At equilibrium $r'_1 = 0$ — the bracket vanishes, recovering the equilibrium constant.

Temperature dependence of $K_e$

The equilibrium constant is a ratio of rate constants

$$K_e = \frac{k_1}{k_{-1}} \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad K_e(T) = K_e(T_0)\, e^{\frac{\Delta H^{\circ}_{rxn}}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_0}-\frac{1}{T}\right)}$$
$E_1 - E_{-1} = \Delta H_{rxn}$barrier difference = heat of reaction
Exothermic $\Delta H < 0$:  $K_e$ falls as $T$ risesvan't Hoff — heat is a product
Endothermic $\Delta H > 0$:  $K_e$ rises as $T$ risesheat is a reactant

Energy landscape

Forward and reverse barriers set $\Delta H_{rxn}$

What is the rate expression for an enzymatic reaction?

The mechanism, pictorially

Substrate binds, reacts, product leaves

Two elementary steps

Adsorption then reaction

$\mathrm{E + S} \underset{k_2}{\overset{k_1}{\rightleftharpoons}} \mathrm{E{\cdot}S}$1 · adsorption — enzyme–substrate complex forms
$\mathrm{E{\cdot}S} \xrightarrow{\;k_3\;} \mathrm{E + P}$2 · reaction — product releases, enzyme recovered
Total enzyme is conserved:  $C_{E_T} = C_E + C_{E{\cdot}S}$  — we can measure $C_{E_T}$, $C_S$, $C_P$, but not the complex.

PSSH on the complex → Michaelis–Menten

Eliminate the complex to get a measurable rate

$$\mathrm{E + S}\;\underset{k_2}{\overset{k_1}{\rightleftharpoons}}\;\mathrm{E{\cdot}S}\;\xrightarrow{\;k_3\;}\;\mathrm{E + P}$$
$r_{E{\cdot}S} = k_1 C_E C_S - (k_2+k_3)C_{E{\cdot}S} = 0$PSSH: complex is at steady state
$C_{E{\cdot}S} = \dfrac{k_1}{k_2+k_3}C_E C_S$,   sub into $C_{E_T}$use the enzyme balance
$$r_P = k_3 C_{E{\cdot}S} = \frac{V_{max}\,C_S}{K_M + C_S}, \quad V_{max}=k_3 C_{E_T},\ K_M=\frac{k_2+k_3}{k_1}$$

At $C_S = K_M$ the rate is exactly $\tfrac12 V_{max}$ (dashed lines). Large $C_S$ → rate saturates at $V_{max}$.

A double-reciprocal plot is a straight line: slope $=K_M/V_{max}$, y-intercept $=1/V_{max}$, x-intercept $=-1/K_M$.

When an inhibitor competes for the enzyme

Two inhibition mechanisms

Competitive

$I$ occupies the active site ⇒ apparent $K_M\!\uparrow$, $V_{max}$ same.

$r_P = \dfrac{V_{max} C_S}{C_S + K_M\!\left(1+\frac{C_I}{K_I}\right)}$

Non-competitive

$I$ binds elsewhere ⇒ $V_{max}\!\downarrow$, $K_M$ same.

$r_P = \dfrac{V_{max}}{1+\frac{C_I}{K_I}}\cdot\dfrac{C_S}{K_M + C_S}$

Rate expression for cell growth

Monod: growth saturates with substrate

$$r_g = \mu\, C_C, \qquad \mu = \mu_{max}\frac{C_S}{K_S + C_S}$$
$r_g$ = cell growth rate, g/(dm³·s)$C_C$ = cell concentration
$\mu_{max}$ = maximum specific growth rate, s⁻¹$K_S$ = half-saturation (Michaelis-like)
Same mathematical form as Michaelis–Menten — concentrations are mass-based (g/L).

Interactive — drag the sliders

Specific growth rate $\;\mu = \dfrac{\mu_{max} C_S}{K_S + C_S}$

$C_S \ll K_S$: first order in $S$.  $C_S \gg K_S$: zero order, $\mu \to \mu_{max}$ (saturation).
$\mu$ reaches $\tfrac12\mu_{max}$ at $C_S=K_S=$ g/L

When too much substrate is toxic

Andrews (Haldane) model

$$\mu(S) = \mu_{max}\,\frac{S}{K_S + S + \dfrac{S^2}{K_I}}$$
Low $S$: $S^2/K_I$ negligible → rises like Monod$K_I$ = inhibition const.
High $S$: $S^2/K_I$ dominates → $\mu$ declinespeak at $S^*=\sqrt{K_S K_I}$

Interactive — compare with plain Monod

The peak-then-decline of substrate inhibition

Grey dashed = Monod (no inhibition). Smaller $K_I$ → stronger inhibition, lower & earlier peak.
optimal $S^{*}=\sqrt{K_S K_I}=$ g/L

Building up the full model

Each factor switches on one behavior

$$\mu(S,O_2,P) = \frac{\mu_{max}}{1+\frac{P}{K_{I,P}}}\cdot\frac{S}{K_S + S + \frac{S^2}{K_I}}\cdot\frac{O_2}{K_O + O_2}$$

$\frac{S}{K_S+S}$ — substrate limiting (saturates)

$+\frac{S^2}{K_I}$ — substrate inhibition (peaks)

$\frac{O_2}{K_O+O_2}$ — oxygen limiting (saturates)

$\frac{1}{1+P/K_{I,P}}$ — product inhibition (falls)

Worked example I · chain reaction

Gas-phase oxidation of ethylene

$$\mathrm{C_2H_4} + \tfrac12\,\mathrm{O_2} \rightarrow \mathrm{C_2H_4O}$$
StepElementary reaction
Initiation$\mathrm{O_2} \xrightarrow{\;k_i\;} 2\,\mathrm{O^{\bullet}}$
Propagation$\mathrm{O^{\bullet}} + \mathrm{C_2H_4} \xrightarrow{\;k_{p2}\;} \mathrm{C_2H_4O^{\bullet}}$
Propagation$\mathrm{C_2H_4O^{\bullet}} + \mathrm{O_2} \xrightarrow{\;k_{p1}\;} \mathrm{C_2H_4O} + \mathrm{O^{\bullet}}$
Termination$\mathrm{O^{\bullet}} + \mathrm{O^{\bullet}} \xrightarrow{\;k_t\;} \mathrm{O_2}$
Initiation = termination gives $C_{\mathrm{O^{\bullet}}} \propto [\mathrm{O_2}]^{1/2}$, so $\;r_{\mathrm{EO}} \propto [\mathrm{C_2H_4}]\,[\mathrm{O_2}]^{1/2}$ — first order in ethylene, half order in oxygen.

Worked example II · bi-substrate enzyme

Lactate dehydrogenase (ordered Bi-Bi)

$$\text{Lactate} + \text{NAD}^{+} \underset{}{\overset{\text{LDH}}{\rightleftharpoons}} \text{Pyruvate} + \text{NADH} + \text{H}^{+}$$
With two substrates $A,B$ binding in order and PSSH on the complexes, what is the initial rate?
$v = \dfrac{V_{max}\,A\,B}{K_{iA}K_{mB} + K_{mB}A + K_{mA}B + AB}$ — reduces to Michaelis–Menten in $B$ when $A$ is saturating.

Worked example III · microorganism

Growth with substrate inhibition

$\mathrm{E + S} \underset{k_{-1}}{\overset{k_1}{\rightleftharpoons}} \mathrm{ES} \xrightarrow{\;k_2\;} \mathrm{E + growth}$productive path
$\mathrm{ES + S} \underset{k_{-3}}{\overset{k_3}{\rightleftharpoons}} \mathrm{ESS}$  (dead-end complex)a 2nd substrate blocks the site at high $S$
$$\mu(S) = \mu_{max}\,\frac{S}{K_S + S + \frac{S^2}{K_I}}$$

Checks:  $S \ll K_S \Rightarrow \mu \propto S$  ·  $S \gg \sqrt{K_S K_I} \Rightarrow \mu \to 0$ (substrate-inhibited).

Key Takeaways

1

Elementary reaction → power law $r = k_r C_A^a C_B^b$; temperature enters through Arrhenius.

2

Non-elementary reaction → propose a mechanism, then use the RLS and PSSH to reach a measurable rate.

3

PSSH on radicals or complexes yields Michaelis–Menten and Monod forms — and non-integer orders like $3/2$.

4

For reversible reactions, $K_e(T)$ follows van't Hoff: it falls (exo) or rises (endo) with $T$.
