Lecture 9-1

How to design an unsteady-state, non-isothermal batch reactor?

Every unsteady non-isothermal reactor = these two, coupled

Mole balance + energy balance, solved together

Reactor mole balance

$$\frac{dN_i}{dt}=F_{i0}-F_i+\sum_j \nu_{ij}\,r_j\,V$$

accumulation = in − out + generation

Reactor energy balance

$$\Big(\textstyle\sum_i N_iC_{p,i}\Big)\frac{dT}{dt}=\dot Q+\dot W_s-\sum_j r_jV\Delta H_{Rxj}-\sum_i F_{i0}C_{p,i}(T-T_F)$$
$T$ enters the mole balance through every rate constant $k_j(T)$ — that is why the two equations cannot be solved separately.

Batch: no inflow, no outflow  ($F_{i0}=F_i=0$)

Batch reactor — mole balance

$$\frac{dN_i}{dt}=\sum_j \nu_{ij}\,r_j\,V$$

Constant volume → work in concentration

$$\frac{dC_i}{dt}=\frac{1}{V}\frac{dN_i}{dt}=\sum_j \nu_{ij}\,r_j$$

The example reaction network

$$\text{(1)}\;\; A+2B \rightleftharpoons C+3D$$
$$\text{(2)}\;\; 2A+C \rightarrow E+F$$

$A$ is consumed by both — its net rate carries both stoichiometries.

Batch: no feed term either

Batch reactor — energy balance

$$\Big(\textstyle\sum_i N_iC_{p,i}\Big)\frac{dT}{dt}=\dot Q+\dot W_s-\sum_j r_jV\,\Delta H_{Rxj}(T)$$
$\dot Q=UA\,\Delta T_{LM}$heat removed through the jacket
$\dot W_s=N_p\rho N^3 D^5$stirrer (shaft) work added to the fluid
$\Delta H_{Rxj}(T)=\Delta H^{\circ}_{Rxj}+\Delta C_{p,j}(T-T_R)$temperature-corrected heat of reaction

Standard tank + conventional jacket, flat bottom

How much heat leaves through the wall?

$$\dot Q=UA\,\Delta T_{LM}=\dot m_{cw}C_{p,cw}(T_{cw,in}-T_{cw,out})$$
$$\Delta T_{LM}=\frac{(T_{cw,in}-T)-(T_{cw,out}-T)}{\ln[(T_{cw,in}-T)/(T_{cw,out}-T)]}$$
$U$ = overall coefficient (wall + two films); for a jacketed tank $A=\pi T_k Z+\tfrac{\pi}{4}T_k^2$ (side wall + flat bottom).
Writing $\dot Q$ two ways gives both the coolant flow $\dot m_{cw}$ and the area $A$ from one solve; the log-mean $\Delta T$ accounts for the coolant warming as it passes.

The impeller does work on the fluid — a heat source

Shaft work = power = $N_p\rho N^3D^5$

$$\dot W_s=N_p\,\rho\,N^3 D^5$$
$$Re=\frac{\rho\,D^2 N}{\mu}\quad(\text{turbulent if } Re>10^4)$$

$N$ = rotational speed (rev/s), $D$ = impeller diameter, $N_p$ = power number (a geometry constant at high $Re$).

Lecture 9-2

How to design an unsteady-state, non-isothermal semi-batch reactor?

One reactant fed continuously → volume grows

Semi-batch reactor — mole balance

Species fed at $F_{A0}$, no outflow

$$\frac{dN_A}{dt}=F_{A0}+\big(\nu_{1A}r_1+\nu_{2A}r_2\big)V$$
$$\frac{dN_i}{dt}=\Big(\sum_j\nu_{ij}r_j\Big)V\quad(i\neq A)$$

Volume is now a state variable

$$\frac{dV}{dt}=v_0\qquad V(0)=V_0$$

Because $V$ changes, always convert with $C_i=N_i/V$ after updating both $N_i$ and $V$.

The feed enters cold — it must be heated to $T$

Semi-batch reactor — energy balance

$$\Big(\textstyle\sum_i N_iC_{p,i}\Big)\frac{dT}{dt}=\dot Q-\sum_j r_jV\Delta H_{Rxj}-\sum_i F_{i0}C_{p,i}\,(T-T_F)$$
$-\sum_i F_{i0}C_{p,i}(T-T_F)$new term: sensible heat to warm the incoming feed from $T_F$ to $T$
$\dot W_s\approx 0$for this example, shaft work ≪ heat of reaction
$\dot Q=UA\,(T_{cool}-T)$jacket removes (or adds) heat toward a coolant temperature

How to determine the stability of a CSTR at the specified operating conditions?

The dangerous minutes are the first ones

Stability & the approach to steady state

  • During startup, how $T$ and $C$ approach their steady-state values matters as much as the values themselves.
  • A significant temperature overshoot can degrade product or reactant.
  • Overshoot may push the reactor into an unsafe region — past its practical stability limit.
The clearest way to see the approach is the temperature–concentration phase plane: plot $T$ against $C_A$ and watch the trajectory spiral into the steady state.

Mixed-flow, but now time-dependent

CSTR — unsteady mole & energy balance

Mole balance

$$\frac{dN_i}{dt}=F_{i0}-F_i+\Big(\sum_j\nu_{ij}r_j\Big)V$$

$F_i=C_iv_0$, $C_i=N_i/V$, $V$ constant.

Energy balance

$$\Big(\sum_iN_iC_{p,i}\Big)\frac{dT}{dt}=\dot Q+\dot W_s-\sum_j r_jV\Delta H_{Rxj}-\sum_iF_{i0}C_{p,i}(T-T_F)$$

  • $V=5\ \text{m}^3$; feed $F_{A0}=3$, $F_{B0}=10\ \text{mol/s}$, $v_0=10\ \text{m}^3/\text{s}$.
  • Initial contents at $T_0=50\,°\text{C}$; feed at $T_F=40\,°\text{C}$.
  • Space time $\tau=V/v_0=0.5\ \text{s}$ — a fast reactor.

How to determine the runaway conditions for a CSTR?

When heat generation outpaces heat removal

Thermal runaway

Highly exothermic reaction + rate of heat removal ≪ rate of heat generation → temperature accelerates, which accelerates the rate, which generates more heat…
  • Heat generated $G(T)$ rises like an S-curve (Arrhenius, saturating at full conversion).
  • Heat removed $R(T)=UA(T-T_a)$ is a straight line in $T$.
  • Runaway when the line no longer intersects the low branch of the S-curve.
$$G(T)=(-\Delta H_{Rx})\,r(T)V$$
$$R(T)=UA\,(T-T_a)$$

Runaway ⇔ $G(T) > R(T)$ for all reachable $T$

Take-home problem — find the tipping point

Mixed-flow reactor: the critical heat-transfer area

$$A\rightarrow B\quad(\text{1st order, exothermic})$$
  • $V=0.20\ \text{m}^3$, $C_{A0}=0.02\ \text{mol/m}^3$, $T_0=85\,°\text{C}$.
  • $\dot m_0=90\ \text{kg/min}$, $T_F=40\,°\text{C}$, $MW_A=90\ \text{g/mol}$, $\rho=900\ \text{kg/m}^3$.
  • Coolant $T_{cool}=35\,°\text{C}$, high flow, $U=120\ \text{W/m}^2\text{K}$.
Find: the critical heat-transfer area $A_{crit}$ below which the reaction runs away and the reactor explodes. $\dot W_s\approx0$; $-\Delta H_{Rx}=250\ \text{J/g}$, $C_p=2\ \text{J/gK}$.

How to compute the unsteady-state, non-isothermal tubular plug-flow reactor?

Now both time and position matter

PFR — unsteady mole balance

Shell balance on a slice $dV$

$$\frac{\partial C_i}{\partial t}=\sum_j\nu_{ij}r_j-\frac{\partial F_i}{\partial V}$$

Differentiated w.r.t. volume

$$\frac{1}{V}\frac{\partial N_i}{\partial t}=\sum_j\nu_{ij}r_j-\frac{\partial F_i}{\partial V}$$

A PDE: $C_i(t,V)$. Accumulation + convective flux − reaction.

$V$ and $t$ are the two independent variables

PFR — unsteady energy balance

$$\Big(\sum_i C_iC_{p,i}\Big)\frac{\partial T}{\partial t}=-\sum_j r_j\Delta H_{Rxj}-\Big(\sum_i F_iC_{p,i}\Big)\frac{\partial T}{\partial V}+\dot Q_a$$
$\dfrac{\partial T}{\partial t}$local heating/cooling — how $T$ at a point evolves in time
$\big(\sum F_iC_{p,i}\big)\dfrac{\partial T}{\partial V}$convection — hot fluid carried downstream
adiabatic here → $\dot Q_a=0$the example bed has no wall cooling

Turn one PDE into many ODEs

The method of lines: slice the reactor

  • Chop the reactor into $n$ nodes of size $\Delta V=V/(n-1)$.
  • Replace the spatial derivative with a backward (upwind) difference: $\dfrac{\partial F_i}{\partial V}\approx\dfrac{F_{i,j}-F_{i,j-1}}{\Delta V}$.
  • Each node then obeys an ODE in time — solve all $n$ together.
$$\frac{1}{\Delta V}\frac{dN_{i,j}}{dt}=\sum_j\nu r-\frac{F_{i,j}-F_{i,j-1}}{\Delta V}$$
Node 1 = inlet boundary condition ($F_i=F_{i0}$, $T=T_F$). Nodes 2…n are integrated. Backward difference because information flows downstream.

Worked example — adiabatic tubular reactor

Unsteady non-isothermal PFR

  • $V=5\ \text{m}^3$ bed; $C_{A0}=0.3$, $C_{B0}=1\ \text{mol/m}^3$.
  • $F_{A0}=3$, $F_{B0}=10\ \text{mol/s}$, $v_0=10\ \text{m}^3/\text{s}$.
  • Feed & initial temperatures 40 °C / 50 °C; adiabatic wall.
  • Discretize with $n=11$ nodes.
Find: the concentration & temperature profiles as a function of both time and bed volume — until the reactor reaches steady state.

How to compute the unsteady-state, non-isothermal fixed-bed (catalytic) reactor?

Same PDE, now over catalyst — plus pressure drop

Fixed-bed reactor — the three coupled fields

Mole

$$\frac{1}{\rho_b}\frac{\partial C_i}{\partial t}=\nu r-\frac{1}{\rho_b}\frac{\partial F_i}{\partial(A_c z)}$$
rates are per mass of catalyst

Energy

$$\big(\textstyle\sum C_iC_{p,i}\big)\frac{\partial T}{\partial t}=\rho_b\Big(-\sum r_j\Delta H_{Rxj}\Big)-\dots$$
adiabatic bed

Momentum

$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial z}=-\beta_0\,\frac{P_0}{P}\frac{T}{T_0}\frac{F_T}{F_{T0}}$$
Ergun pressure drop

Catalyst weight replaces volume: $W=\rho_b A_c z$, with bulk density $\rho_b=(1-\varepsilon)\rho_p$ and cross-section $A_c=v_0/v_s$.

Pressure sets velocity sets residence time

The Ergun equation, node by node

$$\beta_0=\frac{f\,G^2}{\rho_F d_p},\quad f=\frac{1-\varepsilon}{\varepsilon^3}\Big(1.75+\frac{150(1-\varepsilon)}{Re}\Big)$$
$$Re=\frac{G\,d_p}{\mu_F},\qquad G=\frac{\rho_F v_0}{A_c}$$
As $P$ falls, the gas expands, $v$ rises, residence time drops — so pressure, flow and conversion are all coupled.
$$\frac{v}{v_0}=\frac{P_0}{P}\frac{T}{T_0}\frac{F_T}{F_{T0}}\quad(\text{ideal gas})$$

Problem I — from a 100 mL lab reactor to a 50 L plant

Multi-reaction batch with a solid catalyst

Problem I: catalytic conversion of G

$$G\rightarrow H+3W$$$$H+2W\rightarrow L+F$$$$H+F\rightarrow M+W$$

Catalytic (rates per gram of catalyst): $r_{1s}=k_{1s}C_G$, $r_{2s}=k_{2s}C_H$, $r_{3s}=k_{3s}C_H$.

Lab: $V_L=100$ mL, $m_{cat,L}=0.5$ g, at 120 °C: $k_1=0.0015$, $k_2=0.0019$, $k_3=0.0013\ \text{min}^{-1}$; $E_a=64,\,60.8,\,70.2$ kJ/mol.
Plant: $V=50$ L, 3 % catalyst loading. Predict $X_G$, yields $Y_H,Y_L,Y_M$ and selectivity vs time.

The trick: normalize the rate to catalyst mass

Turning a lab $k$ into a catalyst-specific $k_s$

$$V_L\,k_1=m_{cat,L}\,k_{1s0}\;\Rightarrow\;k_{1s0}=\frac{V_L k_1}{m_{cat,L}}$$

$k_{1s0}$ has units of L·min⁻¹·gcat⁻¹ — a property of the catalyst, not the vessel.

  • Mass balance per catalyst mass: $\dfrac{1}{m_{cat}}\dfrac{dN_i}{dt}=\sum_j\nu_{ij}r_{js}$.
  • Plant catalyst load: $m_{cat}=0.03\,(V\!\cdot\!1000)$ g.
  • Same $k_{s0}$, new $V$ and $m_{cat}$ → predicted plant performance.

Higher $T$ speeds every step and drives intermediate $H$ onward — lifting conversion and $L$ yield. But the highest-$E_a$ path (to $M$) accelerates fastest, so pushing $T$ too far tilts the $L{:}M$ split toward $M$.

Why the scale-up works

What Problem I teaches

  • Because $k_{s}$ is normalized to catalyst mass, the same kinetics predict both the 100 mL lab and the 50 L plant — only $V$ and $m_{cat}$ change.
  • Selectivity to $L$ vs $M$ is an Arrhenius competition: the step with the higher $E_a$ wins as $T$ rises, so temperature is the key design handle.
  • Solving mole + energy together catches any exotherm that would shift that competition during operation.

Key Takeaways

1

Unsteady non-isothermal design = mole balance + energy balance in time, always coupled through $k(T)$ and $\Delta H_{Rx}$.

2

Reactor type sets the math: batch/semi-batch/CSTR → ODEs in $t$; PFR & fixed-bed → PDEs solved by the method of lines (add Ergun for gas beds).

3

Watch startup overshoot and the heat-removal vs heat-generation balance — too little cooling means thermal runaway.

4

Normalize rates to catalyst mass and one set of kinetics scales from lab to plant.

In EQUATRAN one skeleton — balances + rate law + integral … by rkv (or nodal (2:n) arrays) — solves every reactor here.
