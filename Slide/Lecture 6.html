Lecture 6-1

How do we do a reactor material / mole balance?

Every reactor design equation is one general mole balance, simplified by the assumptions of an ideal reactor.

Classification

Four ideal reactors, four sets of assumptions

Batch

No in/out flow · well mixed · constant $V$ (liquid) · unsteady.

Semi-batch

Only inlet or outlet · well mixed · $V$ changes · unsteady.

CSTR (mixed flow)

In & out flow · perfectly mixed · steady state, constant $V$.

PFR / Fixed bed

In & out flow · no radial gradient · steady state, plug flow.

Homogeneous or solid-catalytic versions exist for each: normalize the rate per volume ($-r_A$) or per catalyst mass ($-r'_A$).

The master equation

General mole balance on species $j$

$$F_{j0}-F_j+\int_V \sum_{i=1}^{R}\nu_{ij}\,r_i\,dV=\frac{dN_j}{dt}$$
$\underbrace{F_{j0}-F_j}_{\text{in} - \text{out}}$convective terms — zero for a batch reactor
$\int_V \sum_i \nu_{ij} r_i\,dV$generation by all $R$ reactions (sign from $\nu_{ij}$)
$\dfrac{dN_j}{dt}$accumulation — zero at steady state (CSTR, PFR)

Rate & stoichiometry

Net rate of a species in multiple reactions

$$\text{A}+2\text{B}\to 4\text{C}+\text{D}\quad r_1=k_1 C_A C_B^2$$ $$2\text{A}+\text{B}\to \text{U}+3\text{G}\quad r_2=k_2 C_A^2 C_B$$
Net rate = sum over reactions weighted by $\nu_{ij}$:
$$r_A=\nu_{1A}r_1+\nu_{2A}r_2=-r_1-2r_2=\sum_i \nu_{ij}r_i$$
Sign convention
  • $\nu_{ij}<0$ → species is a reactant (consumed)
  • $\nu_{ij}>0$ → species is a product (generated)
  • Subscript $1$ in $r_1$ means reaction no. 1 — keep source notation

Temperature dependence

Arrhenius law and the equilibrium constant

Rate constant

$$k(T)=Ae^{-E/RT}=k(T_0)\,e^{\frac{E}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_0}-\frac1T\right)}$$

Equilibrium constant

$$K_e(T)=K_e(T_1)\,e^{\frac{\Delta H_{rxn}^0}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_1}-\frac1T\right)}$$

The reference form (with $T_0$) is exactly how the EQUATRAN scripts encode k = k10*exp((E1/R)*((1/(T0+273))-(1/(T+273)))).

How do we find the time in an isothermal batch reactor to reach a desired conversion?

Constant volume → the mole balance is a set of ODEs in time. Integrate them (EQUATRAN rkv) to get every concentration profile.

Design equation

Batch reactor: homogeneous and solid-catalytic

Homogeneous, constant $V$
$$\frac{1}{V}\frac{dN_j}{dt}=\sum_i \nu_{ij}r_i\;\Rightarrow\;\frac{dC_j}{dt}=\sum_i \nu_{ij}r_i$$
Solid-catalytic (per catalyst mass $W$)
$$\frac{dN_j}{dt}=\sum_i \nu_{ij}r'_i\,W_{cat},\quad C_j=\frac{N_j}{V}$$
Both are initial-value problems: give $N_{j0}$, integrate in $t$. EQUATRAN writes the initial value with # — e.g. NA#NA0.

Worked example · liquid-phase multiple reaction

Two reactions in a batch reactor

$$\text{A}+2\text{B}\underset{k_2}{\overset{k_1}{\rightleftharpoons}}\text{C}+3\text{D}\qquad 2\text{A}+\text{C}\xrightarrow{k_3}\text{E}+\text{F}$$
$$r_1=k_1C_AC_B^2-k_2C_CC_D^3,\quad r_2=k_3C_A^2C_C$$
Determine the concentration profile of every component.
  • $V=5\ \text{m}^3$ (constant)
  • $C_{A0}=0.3,\ C_{B0}=1\ \text{mol/m}^3$
  • $T=60^\circ\text{C}$, $T_0=40^\circ\text{C}$ (Arrhenius reference)

How do we find the time in an isothermal semi-batch reactor?

One stream is added (or removed) during operation, so the volume is no longer constant — we track moles and volume together.

Design equation · variable volume

Add a feed term and a volume ODE

$$\frac{dN_j}{dt}=F_{j0}+\Big(\textstyle\sum_i \nu_{ij}r_i\Big)V,\qquad \frac{dV}{dt}=v_0$$
$F_{j0}$molar feed of species $j$ — zero for species not fed
$\left(\sum_i\nu_{ij}r_i\right)V$generation uses the current volume $V(t)$, not $V_0$
$C_j=N_j/V$concentration falls both by reaction and by dilution

Worked example

Feed A into a reactor charged with B

$$\text{A}+2\text{B}\rightleftharpoons\text{C}+3\text{D}\qquad 2\text{A}+\text{C}\to\text{E}+\text{F}$$
Determine the concentration profile of each component as A is fed.
  • Initial charge $V_0=5\ \text{m}^3$, $C_{B0}=1\ \text{mol/m}^3$, $C_{A0}=0$
  • Feed of A: $F_{A0}=3$ or $0.3\ \text{mol/s}$
  • Volumetric feed $v_0=0.1\ \text{m}^3/\text{s}$
  • $T=60^\circ\text{C}$

How do we find the volume of an isothermal CSTR for a target conversion?

Steady state + perfect mixing removes the time derivative — the balance becomes a set of algebraic equations.

Design equation · steady state

No accumulation → algebraic balance

$$\frac{F_j-F_{j0}}{V}=\sum_i \nu_{ij}r_i\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad V=\frac{F_{j0}-F_j}{-\sum_i \nu_{ij}r_i}$$
rates $r_i$ evaluated at exit conditionsthe whole tank sits at the outlet composition
$C_j=F_j/v$outlet concentration; $v=v_0$ for a liquid
$\tau=V/v_0$space time — the design unknown we solve for

Worked example · gas/liquid multiple reaction

Find $V$ so that $X_{A1}=0.40$

$$\text{A}+2\text{B}\rightleftharpoons\text{C}+3\text{D}\qquad 2\text{A}+\text{C}\to\text{E}+\text{F}$$
$$k_1=10,\;k_2=5,\;k_3=2$$
Determine $V$ and every exit concentration when conversion of A in reaction 1 is 0.40.
  • $F_{A0}=3\ \text{mol/s}$, $F_{B0}=10\ \text{mol/s}$
  • $v_0=10\ \text{m}^3/\text{s}$

Python — scipy.optimize.fsolve
from scipy.optimize import fsolve
k1,k2,k3,v0=10,5,2,10
FA0,FB0=3,10
def eqs(F,V):
    FA,FB,FC,FD,FE,FF=F
    CA,CB,CC,CD=FA/v0,FB/v0,FC/v0,FD/v0
    r1=k1*CA*CB**2-k2*CC*CD**3
    r2=k3*CA**2*CC
    return [FA-FA0-(-r1-2*r2)*V, FB-FB0-(-2*r1)*V,
            FC-(r1-r2)*V, FD-3*r1*V, FE-r2*V, FF-r2*V]
# outer loop: adjust V until XA1 = 0.40
solveV = lambda V: 0.5*(FB0-fsolve(eqs,[FA0,FB0,0,0,0,0],
                 args=(V,))[1])/FA0 - 0.40

How do we find the concentration profile in a mixed-flow bioreactor?

A chemostat with a predator–prey culture — the same unsteady mixed-flow balance, but the rates are microbial growth kinetics.

Worked example · Alcaligenes (prey) & Colpidium (predator)

Two cells competing on one substrate

$$\text{X}_1+\text{S}\to\text{more X}_1\quad(r_1=\mu_1 C_{X1})$$ $$\text{X}_1+\text{X}_2\to\text{more X}_2\quad(r_2=\mu_2 C_{X2})$$
$\mu_1=\dfrac{\mu_{1m}C_S}{K_S+C_S}$ (prey on substrate), $\mu_2=\dfrac{\mu_{2m}C_{X1}}{K_P+C_{X1}}$ (predator on prey).

How do we find the volume of an isothermal tubular (PFR) reactor?

Steady state, plug flow: the balance is an ODE in volume. For gas phase, the volumetric flow itself changes as moles change.

Design equation · steady state

A differential balance along the length

$$\frac{dF_j}{dV}=\sum_i \nu_{ij}r_i,\qquad \tau=\frac{V}{v_0},\qquad C_j=\frac{F_j}{v}$$
integrate from $V=0$ (inlet) to the exitan initial-value problem in $V$, solved with rkv
$F_j(0)=F_{j0}$feed molar flow rates set the initial condition

Why gas reactors are different

Volumetric flow changes with moles, $P$ and $T$

Liquid phase
$$v=v_0\;\;\Rightarrow\;\;C_j=\frac{F_j}{v_0}$$
Gas phase (ideal)
$$v=v_0\left(\frac{P_0}{P}\right)\!\left(\frac{T}{T_0}\right)\!\left(\frac{F_T}{F_{T0}}\right)$$
These two reactions produce more moles than they consume ($F_T\uparrow$), so a gas stream expands — diluting concentrations and needing a larger $V$ than the liquid case.

Worked example

Same reactions, now in a PFR

$$\text{A}+2\text{B}\rightleftharpoons\text{C}+3\text{D}\quad 2\text{A}+\text{C}\to\text{E}+\text{F}$$
$$k_1=10,\;k_2=5,\;k_3=2$$
Find the PFR volume & exit concentrations for $X_{A1}=0.40$.
  • $F_{A0}=3$, $F_{B0}=10\ \text{mol/s}$, $v_0=10\ \text{m}^3/\text{s}$
  • Compare liquid vs gas phase

from scipy.integrate import solve_ivp
k1,k2,k3,v0=10,5,2,10; FA0,FB0=3,10

def rhs(V,F,gas):

    v=v0*(FT/FT0) if gas else v0
    CA,CB,CC,CD=[f/v for f in F[:4]]
    r1=k1*CA*CB**2-k2*CC*CD**3
    r2=k3*CA**2*CC
    return [-r1-2*r2,-2*r1,r1-r2,3*r1,r2,r2]
sol=solve_ivp(rhs,[0,2],[FA0,FB0,0,0,0,0],

How do we find the weight of catalyst in an isothermal fixed-bed reactor?

A PFR packed with solid catalyst. Two new ingredients: rate per catalyst mass, and pressure drop through the bed (Ergun).

Definitions

Bed density, particle density & void fraction

Void fraction

$$\Phi=\frac{\text{voids}}{\text{total bed}}\approx0.38\text{–}0.42$$

Bed density

$$\rho_b=(1-\Phi)\rho_c$$

Design equation

$$\frac{dF_j}{dW}=\sum_i \nu_{ij}r'_i$$

$W=\rho_b V_{bed}$ ties catalyst weight to bed volume. Rates $r'_i$ are per kg of catalyst (mol · kg⁻¹ · s⁻¹).

Two more tools

Space velocity and the pressure-drop equation

Space velocity (throughput per catalyst)
  • LHSV $=\dfrac{v_\text{liq}}{V_\text{bed}}$, GHSV $=\dfrac{v_\text{gas}}{V_\text{bed}}$
  • WHSV $=\dfrac{\dot m_\text{feed}}{W_\text{cat}}$
Ergun equation — pressure drop
$$\frac{dP}{dz}=-\frac{G}{\rho\,d_p}\frac{(1-\Phi)}{\Phi^3}\left[\frac{150(1-\Phi)\mu}{d_p}+1.75\,G\right]$$

$G=\dot m/A_c=\rho_0 u_0$ is the mass flux.

Worked example · gas-phase multiple reaction in an FBR

  • $F_{A0}=3$, $F_{B0}=10\ \text{mol/s}$, $u_0=15\ \text{m/s}$
  • $d_p=5\ \text{mm}$, $\Phi=0.50$, $\rho_c=1200\ \text{kg/m}^3$
  • $P_F=20\ \text{bar}$, $\mu=0.03\ \text{cP}$; $M_{w}$: A 50, B 32, I 28

When can we skip the solver and use an analytical solution?

For a single reaction with an apparent rate law, each ideal reactor has a closed-form concentration–conversion relation.

Single reaction · apparent rate $-r_A=kC_A^n$

Closed-form $C_A/C_{A0}$ by reactor and order

OrderBatch / PFR (time or $\tau$)CSTR (steady)
1st$\dfrac{C_A}{C_{A0}}=e^{-k\tau}$$\dfrac{C_A}{C_{A0}}=\dfrac{1}{1+k\tau}$
2nd$\dfrac{C_A}{C_{A0}}=\dfrac{1}{1+k\tau C_{A0}}$$C_A=\dfrac{-1+\sqrt{1+4k\tau C_{A0}}}{2k\tau}$
$n$th$\dfrac{C_A}{C_{A0}}=\big[1+(n-1)C_{A0}^{\,n-1}k\tau\big]^{\frac{1}{1-n}}$solve $\;x+\mathrm{Da}\,x^n=1$
$\tau=V/v_0$ (flow) or $t$ (batch). These are the exact solutions the numerical rkv run must reproduce — a great sanity check.

Gas phase only

The expansion factor $\varepsilon$ handles volume change

$$\varepsilon=\frac{\sum\nu_i/\nu_A\;F_{A0}}{F_{I0}+F_0}=y_{A0}\,\delta$$
For $\text{A}\to 4\text{R}$ with pure A feed: $\varepsilon=\dfrac{(4-1)}{-1}\times\dfrac{1}{1}\!=\!3$ → the gas quadruples in volume at full conversion.
Add 50% inert ($F_{I0}=F_{A0}$): $\varepsilon=3\times\tfrac12=1.5$.
$$v=v_0(1+\varepsilon X)$$

Bigger $\varepsilon$ → more dilution → conversion is harder to reach.

How do we design when external mass transfer limits the rate?

The reactant must first travel from the bulk gas to the catalyst surface. Two resistances in series — reaction and film diffusion.

Apparent rate with a film

Reaction and mass transfer in series

$$r'=\frac{k'_r\,k_c a\,C_{Ab}}{k'_r+k_c a}\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad \frac{1}{k_\text{eff}}=\frac{1}{k'_r}+\frac{1}{k_c a}$$
RegimeConditionApparent rateWhat controls
Reaction controlled$k_c a\gg k'_r$$r'=k'_r C_{Ab}$chemistry / temperature
MT controlled$k_c a\ll k'_r$$r'=k_c a\,C_{Ab}$flow, particle size

Derivation

Conversion in a mass-transfer-limited bed

$$\frac{dF_A}{dW}=-k_c a\,C_A,\quad \varepsilon_A=0\Rightarrow v=v_0$$
$$\ln\frac{1}{1-X}=\frac{k_c a\,\rho_b L}{u}$$
From correlations $Sh=2+0.6\,Re^{1/2}Sc^{1/3}$:
$$k_c a\;\propto\;\frac{u^{1/2}}{d_p^{3/2}}$$

So $\ln\frac{1}{1-X}\propto \dfrac{L}{u^{1/2}d_p^{3/2}}$ — smaller particles help, faster flow hurts (per pass).

Worked example · trace Cl₂ scrubbing

Can we push 63.2% removal higher?

Currently $X=0.632$ (so $k_c a\rho_b L/u=1$). Proposal: increase $u\times4$, decrease $d_p\times3$, increase $L\times1.5$. New removal?
$$\frac{E_\text{new}}{E_\text{old}}=f_L\,f_u^{-1/2}\,f_d^{-3/2}$$
$E_\text{new}=1\cdot(1.5)(4)^{-1/2}(1/3)^{-3/2}=3.90$
$X=1-e^{-3.90}=\mathbf{0.98}$ — removal rises from 63% to ~98%. Smaller particles dominate the gain.

How do we design when internal (pore) diffusion limits the rate?

Reactant must diffuse into the porous pellet. The effectiveness factor $\eta$ discounts the intrinsic rate for the concentration gradient inside.

Thiele modulus & effectiveness

How much of the pellet actually works?

$$\eta=\frac{3}{\phi^2}\big(\phi\coth\phi-1\big),\quad \phi=R\sqrt{\frac{k\rho_c}{D_e}}$$
$\phi\ll1$: whole pellet reacts, $\eta\to1$.
$\phi\gg1$: only the outer shell reacts, $\eta\to 3/\phi$.
$\phi$ = (reaction rate) / (diffusion rate). Big pellets, fast reaction, or slow diffusion → big $\phi$ → small $\eta$.
$$r'_\text{obs}=\eta\,k\,C_{As}^n$$

$n$th-order, strong internal resistance

The apparent order shifts to $(n+1)/2$

$$r'_1=\left(\frac{2}{n+1}\right)^{1/2}\frac{3}{R}\sqrt{\frac{k'_n D_e}{\rho_c}}\;C_{Ab}^{\,(n+1)/2}$$
$\eta=\left(\dfrac{2}{n+1}\right)^{1/2}\dfrac{3}{R}\sqrt{\dfrac{D_e}{k'_n\rho_c}}\;C_{Ab}^{\,(1-n)/2}$the effectiveness factor, concentration-dependent for $n\ne1$
observed order $=\dfrac{n+1}{2}$a true 2nd-order reaction looks 1.5-order from outside
