Lecture 1-1

What is the difference between the reaction rate, the consuming rate of a reactant, and the generating rate of a product?

Starting point

A single reaction and its stoichiometry

$$\nu_{1A}\,\mathrm{A} + \nu_{1B}\,\mathrm{B} \;\longrightarrow\; \nu_{1C}\,\mathrm{C} + \nu_{1D}\,\mathrm{D}$$

Sign convention

ν < 0 reactant   ν > 0 product

Limiting reactant

Set its coefficient to a reference: $\nu_{1A}=-1$ if A is limiting.

Notation

Subscript 1 in $\nu_{1A}$ means reaction no. 1.

Consuming rate of a reactant

Counting molecules is not enough

$$\text{Consuming rate of A} \;=\; -\frac{dN_A}{dt}\quad\left[\frac{\text{mol}}{\text{s}}\right]$$
This extensive quantity grows with the size of the system — a bigger reactor consumes more A per second even for identical chemistry.
Fix it by dividing by an amount of "reaction space" → an intensive, specific rate that is a property of the chemistry, not the equipment.

Specific consuming rate

One rate, three ways to normalize

Per fluid volume

$-r_A=-\dfrac{1}{V}\dfrac{dN_A}{dt}$

$\left[\dfrac{\text{mol A}}{\text{m}^3\cdot\text{s}}\right]$

Homogeneous (single phase)

Per catalyst mass

$-r'_A=-\dfrac{1}{W}\dfrac{dN_A}{dt}$

$\left[\dfrac{\text{mol A}}{\text{kg}\cdot\text{s}}\right]$

Solid-catalyzed reaction

Per catalyst area

$-r''_A=-\dfrac{1}{S}\dfrac{dN_A}{dt}$

$\left[\dfrac{\text{mol A}}{\text{m}^2\cdot\text{s}}\right]$

Surface-limited reaction

What is the reaction rate?

The reaction rate is tied to the limiting reactant

$$r_1=\frac{1}{\nu_{1A}}\,r_A=-r_A=-\frac{1}{V}\frac{dN_A}{dt}$$

Homogeneous

$r_1=-r_A$

Per catalyst mass

$r'_1=-r'_A$

Per catalyst area

$r''_1=-r''_A$

With $\nu_{1A}=-1$, the reaction rate equals the specific consuming rate of the limiting reactant — always positive.

What is the reaction rate in a multiple-reaction system?

Multiple reaction · conversion

When A reacts in more than one reaction

$\mathrm{A}+2\mathrm{B}\rightarrow 4\mathrm{C}+\mathrm{D}$  (1 · main)
$2\mathrm{A}+\mathrm{B}\rightarrow \mathrm{U}+3\mathrm{G}$  (2 · side)
$3\mathrm{B}+2\mathrm{C}\rightarrow \mathrm{E}+5\mathrm{Y}$  (3 · side)
A is the limiting reactant; C is the desired product.
$$X_{A1}=\frac{\text{mol A reacted in rxn 1}}{\text{initial mol A}}$$
$$X_{A2}=\frac{\text{mol A reacted in rxn 2}}{\text{initial mol A}}$$
$$X_A=\frac{\text{total mol A reacted}}{\text{initial mol A}}=X_{A1}+X_{A2}$$

Multiple reaction · yield & selectivity

How much of the reacted A becomes the desired C?

QuantityDefinition (relative to A)Reads as
Conversion $X_A$$\dfrac{C_{A0}-C_A}{C_{A0}}$fraction of A that reacted
Yield $Y$$\dfrac{C_C/\nu_{1C}}{C_{A0}/(-\nu_{1A})}$C made per A fed
Selectivity $S$$\dfrac{C_C/\nu_{1C}}{(C_{A0}-C_A)/(-\nu_{1A})}$C made per A reacted
Text-book $S_{D/U}$$\dfrac{C_C/\nu_{1C}}{C_U/(\nu_{2U}/\nu_{2A})}$desired ÷ undesired

The three are linked by one identity:  $Y = S \times X_A$.

One variable tracks the whole reaction

Extent of reaction $\xi$

$$\xi=\frac{N_i-N_{i0}}{\nu_i}\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad \Delta N_i=\nu_i\,\xi\quad[\text{mol}]$$
$\xi$ is the same for every species in one reactionone "turnover" counter, in mol
Reactants ($\nu_i<0$) are consumed; products ($\nu_i>0$) are formedsign follows stoichiometry
Multiple reactions: each reaction $j$ has its own extent, so $\Delta N_i=\displaystyle\sum_j \nu_{ij}\,\xi_j$the $\xi_1,\xi_2$ used next
A reacted $=-\nu_{A}\,\xi$, so conversion follows directly: $\;X_A=\dfrac{-\nu_A\,\xi}{N_{A0}}$ — extent feeds $X$, $Y$, and $S$.

Worked example · with numbers

Splitting conversion between the main and side reaction

Feed 100 mol A. Reaction 1 consumes 30 mol A ($X_{A1}=0.30$); reaction 2 consumes 20 mol A ($X_{A2}=0.20$). For R1 $\mathrm{A}+2\mathrm{B}\rightarrow 4\mathrm{C}+\mathrm{D}$, find the overall conversion, the mol of C formed, and the selectivity of A to the main route.
$X_A=X_{A1}+X_{A2}=0.30+0.20=0.50$conversions add across reactions
$N_C=\dfrac{\nu_{1C}}{-\nu_{1A}}(30)=4\times 30=120$ mol4 mol C per mol A in R1
$S_{\text{main}}=\dfrac{30}{50}=0.60,\qquad Y=S\times X_A=0.30$main route ÷ total A reacted

Reaction rate · multiple reactions

A species' net rate sums over every reaction

$$r_i=\sum_{j}\nu_{ji}\,r_j$$
$r_A=\nu_{1A}r_1+\nu_{2A}r_2=-r_1-2r_2$A consumed by rxn 1 & 2
$r_B=\nu_{1B}r_1+\nu_{2B}r_2+\nu_{3B}r_3=-2r_1-r_2-3r_3$B consumed by all three
$r_C=\nu_{1C}r_1+\nu_{3C}r_3=+4r_1-2r_3$C made by rxn 1, eaten by rxn 3

Glycerol → glycerol carbonate → glycidol

Glycerol carbonate is the desired product but also an intermediate. Which knob most threatens its yield — the reverse step $k_{-1}$ or the decomposition $k_3$?

Official Quiz 1 · open questions

Explain in your own words

1. (1-1) How are the consuming rate, the generation rate, and the reaction rate related?

All scale with one reaction rate via stoichiometry: $-r_A=-\nu_{1A}r_1$, $r_C=\nu_{1C}r_1$; the reaction rate equals the specific consuming rate of the limiting reactant ($r_1=-r_A$ when $\nu_{1A}=-1$).

2. (1-1) Why is the reaction rate sometimes referenced to volume, sometimes to catalyst mass?

The basis matches the reacting space: fluid volume for homogeneous reactions, catalyst mass or surface area for solid-catalyzed ones — so the specific rate stays intensive.

3. (1-2) In multiple reactions, how is a reactant's consumption rate related across reactions?

Sum over reactions: $r_A=\sum_j \nu_{jA}\,r_j$ — each reaction contributes its coefficient times its own rate.

Key Takeaways

1

Make rates intensive: divide $-dN_A/dt$ by volume, catalyst mass, or area to match the reaction type.

2

Every species rate is one reaction rate scaled by stoichiometry: $\;r_i=\nu_{1i}\,r_1$.

3

In multiple reactions, sum over reactions: $\;r_i=\sum_j \nu_{ji}\,r_j$, and conversions add: $X_A=\sum X_{Aj}$.

4

Remember the identity $Y=S\times X$ — and that a reactive intermediate makes yield peak, then fall.
