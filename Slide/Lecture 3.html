Lecture 3-1

What is the elementary step in a solid catalytic reaction?

A real system — glycerol carbonate production

The reaction happens inside a solid pellet

$$\text{DMC} + \text{Glycerol} \xrightarrow{\text{catalyst}} \text{Glycerol carbonate} + 2\,\text{CH}_3\text{OH}$$
Only the bulk concentration $C_{Ab}$ can be measured — yet the rate is set by what happens on sites inside the pellet.

Interactive 1 — click a step to follow molecule A

A molecule's journey: 7 elementary steps

The whole picture — what happens at each step

From bulk fluid to active site and back

1,7 external & 2,6 internal diffusion are physical; 3 adsorption, 4 surface reaction, 5 desorption are chemical.

Why adsorption & desorption count as chemical steps

It is chemisorption — real bonds form

A reactant does not merely touch the surface; it forms a chemical bond with an active site (chemisorption).
  • Physisorption = weak van der Waals — reversible, no reaction.
  • Chemisorption = a surface chemical bond — this is a reaction, so its rate is a chemical rate.
  • Example: ethylidene chemisorbed on platinum (Pt).

Grouping the seven steps

Two families of steps

Chemical rate steps

3 · Adsorption   $A+S \rightleftharpoons A\!\cdot\!S$

4 · Surface reaction   $A\!\cdot\!S \rightleftharpoons B\!\cdot\!S$

5 · Desorption   $B\!\cdot\!S \rightleftharpoons B+S$

Bonds form / break — chemistry. This lecture's focus.

Physical rate steps

1, 7 · External mass transfer

2, 6 · Internal (pore) mass transfer

Transport only — momentum, heat, mass. No reaction.

Chemical-rate-controlled regime: transport is fast, so the slowest chemical step sets the rate.

Lecture 3-2

What is the rate expression of each chemical-rate elementary step?

Step 3 — reversible

Adsorption: $A + S \rightleftharpoons A\!\cdot\!S$

$r'_{a}=k'_a\,C_A C_S$Attachment: A meets a vacant site $S$
$r'_{d}=k'_d\,C_{A\cdot S}$De-attachment of the adsorbed pair
$r'_{ad}=k'_a\!\left(C_A C_S-\dfrac{C_{A\cdot S}}{K_A}\right),\quad K_A=\dfrac{k'_a}{k'_d}$Net rate = forward − reverse
For a gas phase, use partial pressure: $C_A \to P_A = C_A RT$.

Interactive 2 — surface coverage at equilibrium

Langmuir isotherm: how full is the surface?

$\theta_A=\dfrac{K_A P_A}{1+K_A P_A}$
Strong adsorption (large $K_A$) fills the surface at low pressure. As $P_A\to\infty$, $\theta_A\to1$ — every site occupied.
fractional coverage θ_A vs partial pressure P_A

Interactive 3 — adsorption weakens as it gets hotter

Equilibrium constant vs temperature (exothermic adsorption)

$K_e(T)=K_e(T_1)\,e^{\frac{\Delta H^{\circ}_{ads}}{R}\left(\frac{1}{T_1}-\frac{1}{T}\right)}$
Adsorption is exothermic ($\Delta H^{\circ}_{ads}<0$), so $K_e$ falls as $T$ rises — high temperature drives molecules off the surface.
equilibrium constant K_e vs temperature T (K)

Step 4 — three mechanisms

Surface reaction: how many sites?

MechanismElementary stepRate expression
Single site$A\!\cdot\!S \rightleftharpoons B\!\cdot\!S$$r'_S=k'_S\!\left(C_{A\cdot S}-\dfrac{C_{B\cdot S}}{K_S}\right)$
Dual site$A\!\cdot\!S + S \rightleftharpoons B\!\cdot\!S + S$$r'_S=k'_S\!\left(C_{A\cdot S}C_S-\dfrac{C_{B\cdot S}C_S}{K_S}\right)$
Eley–Rideal$A\!\cdot\!S + B(g) \rightleftharpoons C\!\cdot\!S$$r'_S=k'_S\!\left(C_{A\cdot S}P_B-\dfrac{C_{C\cdot S}}{K_S}\right)$
Eley–Rideal = an adsorbed molecule reacts with a molecule still in the gas phase.

Step 4 — dual-site, three sub-cases

Dual-site reactions come in three flavors

2.1 · Same species

$A\!\cdot\!S + S \rightleftharpoons B\!\cdot\!S + S$

Adsorbed A hops to an adjacent vacant site.

2.2 · Two adsorbed reactants

$A\!\cdot\!S + B\!\cdot\!S \rightleftharpoons C\!\cdot\!S + D\!\cdot\!S$

Both reactants on the surface (true Langmuir–Hinshelwood).

2.3 · Different site types

$A\!\cdot\!S + B\!\cdot\!S^{*} \rightleftharpoons \dots$

Two kinds of site $S$ and $S^{*}$ (e.g. Cu/ZnO).

Each extra adsorbed species that participates adds a $C_{i\cdot S}$ factor — and later, a term in the denominator.

The bookkeeping that closes every derivation

Site balance

$$C_t = C_S + \sum_i C_{i\cdot S} = C_S + C_{A\cdot S} + C_{B\cdot S} + \dots$$
$C_t$total active sites (mol / g cat) — a constant of the catalyst
$C_S$vacant (empty) sites
$C_{i\cdot S}$sites occupied by species $i$

Step 5 — reversible

Desorption: $B\!\cdot\!S \rightleftharpoons B + S$

$$r'_{D}=k'_D\!\left(C_{B\cdot S}-\dfrac{C_S P_B}{K_{DB}}\right)=k'_D\!\left(C_{B\cdot S}-K_B C_S P_B\right)$$

Desorption is adsorption run backwards, so $K_B = 1/K_{DB}$.

Together with the site balance, these three rate laws are all we need to derive any solid-catalytic rate expression.

Worked example — the running case

Cumene decomposition mechanism

$$\underbrace{C_6H_5CH(CH_3)_2}_{C} \longrightarrow \underbrace{C_6H_6}_{B} + \underbrace{C_3H_6}_{P} \qquad C \to B + P$$
Write the three elementary chemical steps and their rate expressions.
Adsorption: $C+S \rightleftharpoons C\!\cdot\!S$$r'_{ad}=k'_a\!\left(\tfrac{P_C C_S}{RT}-\tfrac{C_{C\cdot S}}{K_C}\right)$
Surface: $C\!\cdot\!S \rightleftharpoons B\!\cdot\!S + P(g)$$r'_S=k'_S\!\left(C_{C\cdot S}-\tfrac{C_{B\cdot S}P_P}{K_S}\right)$
Desorption: $B\!\cdot\!S \rightleftharpoons B+S$$r'_D=k'_D\!\left(C_{B\cdot S}-K_B C_S P_B\right)$

Lecture 3-3

What is the rate expression when adsorption / desorption is the limiting step?

The key simplification

Chemical rate controlled: physical rate ≫ chemical rate

$$\text{External \& internal diffusion fast}\;\Rightarrow\; C_{Ab}=C_{As}=C_A$$

No concentration gradient — the surface "sees" the bulk concentration directly.

The observed rate now equals the rate of the slowest chemical step. Find that step, then write its rate in measurable quantities ($P$ or $C$ in the bulk).

A repeatable recipe

How to build any rate expression

1

Pick the rate-limiting step (RLS) — the slowest step. Every other step is assumed at equilibrium ($r=0$).

2

Apply PSSH / QSSA — surface intermediates are reactive; set their net rate to zero to eliminate $C_{i\cdot S}$.

3

Close with the site balance $\,C_t = C_S+\sum C_{i\cdot S}$, solve for $C_S$, and substitute.

Step 4 (later): compare with experimental data and regress the kinetic parameters.

Case A — adsorption controls

Adsorption is the rate-limiting step

Reaction (RLS): $C+S \rightleftharpoons C\!\cdot\!S$rate $\;r'_{ad}=k'_A\!\left(P_C C_S-\tfrac{C_{C\cdot S}}{K_A}\right)$
$$-r'_C=\dfrac{C_t k'_A\!\left(P_C-\tfrac{P_B P_P}{K_P}\right)}{1+\tfrac{K_B P_B P_P}{K_S}+K_B P_B}$$
Linearized (initial, $P_B=P_P=0$): $\;-r'_{C0}=C_t k_A P_{C0}=k^*P_{C0}$ → straight line, slope $k^*$.

How the final rate is obtained — step by step

Adsorption RLS: the full derivation

1. Rate = the slow step$-r'_C=k'_A\!\left(P_C C_S-\tfrac{C_{C\cdot S}}{K_A}\right)$
2. Other steps at equilibrium$C_{C\cdot S}=\tfrac{C_{B\cdot S}P_P}{K_S},\;\; C_{B\cdot S}=K_B P_B C_S$
3. Site balance → solve $C_S$$C_S=\dfrac{C_t}{1+\tfrac{K_B P_B P_P}{K_S}+K_B P_B}$
4. Substitute $C_S$ back$-r'_C=\dfrac{C_t k'_A\!\left(P_C-\tfrac{P_B P_P}{K_P}\right)}{1+\tfrac{K_B P_B P_P}{K_S}+K_B P_B}$

Case C — desorption controls

Desorption is the rate-limiting step

Reaction (RLS): $B\!\cdot\!S \rightleftharpoons B+S$rate $\;r'_D=k'_D\!\left(C_{B\cdot S}-K_B C_S P_B\right)$
$$-r'_{C}=\dfrac{C_t k'_D K_S K_C\!\left(P_C-\tfrac{P_B P_P}{K_P}\right)}{1+K_C P_C + K_S K_C P_C}$$
Linearized (initial): $\;-r'_{C0}\approx\dfrac{k'_D K_S K_C P_{C0}}{K_S K_C P_{C0}}=k'_D$ → constant, independent of $P_{C0}$.

Lecture 3-4

What is the rate expression when the surface reaction is the limiting step?

Case B — surface reaction controls (≈75% of real catalysts)

Surface reaction is the rate-limiting step

$$-r'_C=\dfrac{C_t k'_S K_C\!\left(P_C-\tfrac{P_B P_P}{K_P}\right)}{1+K_C P_C+K_B P_B}$$
Initial rate: $\;-r'_{C0}=\dfrac{C_t k'_S K_C P_{C0}}{1+K_C P_{C0}}$ → rises, then saturates (Langmuir shape).
Low $P$: first order in $P_{C0}$.   High $P$: zero order — the surface is full.

Where the denominator comes from

Deriving the surface-reaction rate law

$-r'_C=k'_S\!\left(C_{C\cdot S}-\tfrac{C_{B\cdot S}P_P}{K_S}\right)$start: rate of the limiting surface step
$C_{C\cdot S}=K_C P_C C_S,\quad C_{B\cdot S}=K_B P_B C_S$adsorption & desorption at equilibrium
$C_S=\dfrac{C_t}{1+K_C P_C+K_B P_B}$site balance → solve for $C_S$
$-r'_C=\dfrac{C_t k'_S K_C\!\left(P_C-\tfrac{P_B P_P}{K_P}\right)}{1+K_C P_C+K_B P_B}$substitute → final rate law
$\dfrac{P_{C0}}{-r'_{C0}}=\dfrac{1}{C_t k'_S K_C}+\dfrac{P_{C0}}{C_t k'_S}$linearized form for regression

Interactive 5 — the Langmuir curve and its linear plot

Saturating rate → a straight line for regression

initial rate −r′(C0) vs P(C0)

Step 4 of the method

Compare with experimental data

$$\dfrac{P_{C0}}{-r'_{C0}}=\dfrac{1}{C_t k'_S K_C}+\dfrac{P_{C0}}{C_t k'_S}$$
Plotting $P_{C0}/(-r'_{C0})$ vs $P_{C0}$ gives a straight line: the slope and intercept return $k'_S$ and $K_C$.
If the data does not fit, the assumed RLS or mechanism is wrong — pick another and repeat.

Lecture 3-5

What is the Langmuir–Hinshelwood / Hougen–Watson rate expression?

The general form (surface reaction limiting)

Langmuir–Hinshelwood / Hougen–Watson

$$-r'_A=\dfrac{k'_r\,C_A^{a}\,C_B^{b}}{\left(1+K_A C_A+K_B C_B+\dots\right)^{n}}$$

Numerator

Driving force — the reactants being consumed.

Denominator terms

Each species that sits on a site during reaction.

Power $n$

Number of different sites in the limiting step.

Guidelines for reading & writing the rate law

Three general rules

1

A species' pressure/concentration in the denominator ⇒ that species is adsorbed on a site during the reaction.

2

The power of the denominator = the number of different sites involved in the rate-limiting step ($n=2$ ⇒ dual site).

3

About 75% of real catalytic reactions are surface-reaction limited — so this is usually the first case to try.

Interactive 6 — why a rate maximum reveals a dual-site mechanism

Single site saturates; dual site peaks

Single: $\;\dfrac{kKC}{1+KC}$
Dual: $\;\dfrac{kKC}{(1+KC)^2}$
A rate that goes through a maximum in a reactant ⇒ it blocks a second site (dual-site). The MEK-from-butanol clue.
rate vs reactant concentration C

Interactive 7 — two species fighting for the same sites

Adding more of B can slow the reaction

$-r'_A=\dfrac{k\,K_A P_A}{\left(1+K_A P_A+K_B P_B\right)^2}$
B occupies sites without reacting — raising $P_B$ or $K_B$ pushes the whole $-r'_A$ curve down. This is inhibition by a spectator.

What is the rate expression with catalyst deactivation?

Catalysts age — activity is not constant

Three ways a catalyst dies

Sintering

Loss of active surface area — pores close, metal crystallites agglomerate.

2nd-order decay

Poisoning

A poison chemisorbs irreversibly on active sites, removing them.

1st-order decay

Coking

Carbonaceous coke deposits on and blocks the surface.

$a=(1+\alpha t)^{-1/2}$

Activity $a(t)\in[0,1]$ scales the fresh rate: $\;-r'_A(t)=a(t)\,\bigl(-r'_{A,\text{fresh}}\bigr)$.

The decay-rate law $\;r_d=-\tfrac{da}{dt}=k_d\,a^{\,n}$

Functional forms of activity decay

Order $n$Differential formIntegral form $a(t)$Typical cause
0 (linear)$-\tfrac{da}{dt}=k_d$$a=1-k_d t$—
1 (exponential)$-\tfrac{da}{dt}=k_d a$$a=e^{-k_d t}$poisoning
2 (reciprocal)$-\tfrac{da}{dt}=k_d a^2$$a=\dfrac{1}{1+k_d t}$sintering
Coking often gives $a=(1+\alpha t)^{-1/2}$ (from $C_c=A t^{n}$ coke build-up).

Reaction → rate → linearized form

Rate expression with deactivation

$$-r'_A(t)=a(t)\,\dfrac{k'\,C_A C_B}{1+K_A C_A+K_B C_B}$$
MechanismDeactivation reactionDecay rateLinearized plot
Poisoning$P+S \to P\!\cdot\!S$$-\tfrac{da}{dt}=k_d a$$\ln a=-k_d t$
Sintering$S+S \to S_2$ (agglomerate)$-\tfrac{da}{dt}=k_d a^2$$\tfrac{1}{a}=1+k_d t$
The linear plot gives the decay order & $k_d$; the fresh LH–HW form is just scaled by $a(t)$.

Key Takeaways

1

Seven steps; three are chemical (adsorption, surface reaction, desorption). Chemical-rate control ⇒ $C_{Ab}=C_A$.

2

Build any rate law: choose the RLS → equilibrate the rest → apply PSSH → close with the site balance → regress.

3

Initial-rate signature identifies the RLS: linear = adsorption, saturating = surface reaction, flat = desorption.

4

LH–HW: denominator lists adsorbed species; its power = number of sites. A maximum ⇒ dual-site; a $\sqrt{P}$ ⇒ dissociation.

5

Real catalysts decay: $-r'_A(t)=a(t)(-r'_{A,\text{fresh}})$. Add the decay term to fit aging data.
