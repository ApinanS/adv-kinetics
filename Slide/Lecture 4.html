The big picture

Seven elementary steps of a solid-catalyzed reaction

A reactant must travel into the catalyst, react, and its product must travel back out. The slowest step is the rate-limiting step.

  • 1, 7 — External diffusion (bulk ⇄ surface) physical
  • 2, 6 — Internal diffusion (in/out of pores) physical
  • 3, 4, 5 — Adsorption · Surface reaction · Desorption chemical
Lecture 3 covered the chemical steps (3–5). Lecture 4 tackles the physical transport steps (1, 2, 6, 7).
4-1

Lecture 4-1

How do we determine the external mass-transfer rate (convection)?

Transport of A from the bulk fluid to the outer surface of the catalyst — a purely physical step driven by a concentration difference.

Lecture 4-1 · convection

External mass transfer: bulk → surface

$$M_A = k_c A\,(C_{Ab}-C_{As})\qquad W_A=\dfrac{M_A}{A}=k_c\,(C_{Ab}-C_{As})$$

$k_c$ — MT coefficient

Units $[\text{m/s}]$. Set by hydrodynamics: velocity, particle size, fluid properties.

Driving force

$C_{Ab}-C_{As}$: the bulk-to-surface gap. No gap ⇒ no transfer.

$W_A$ — molar flux

$\left[\tfrac{\text{mol A}}{\text{m}^2\cdot\text{s}}\right]$ — rate per unit outer area.

Lecture 4-1 · recap from Lecture 3

The chemical rate (surface-reaction control)

Per catalyst mass

$-r'_A=\dfrac{k'_r C_A}{1+K_A C_A+K_B C_B}\;\left[\tfrac{\text{mol A}}{\text{kg}\cdot\text{s}}\right]$

Per catalyst area

$-r''_A=\dfrac{k''_r C_A}{1+K_A C_A+K_B C_B}\;\left[\tfrac{\text{mol A}}{\text{m}^2\cdot\text{s}}\right]$

$$\text{weak adsorption: }K_A C_A+K_B C_B\ll1\;\Rightarrow\;-r'_A=k'_{r,n}C_A^{\,n}\qquad k'_r=S_a\,k''_r$$

$S_a=$ surface area per mass of catalyst $[\text{m}^2/\text{kg}]$. In this lecture we mostly use the first-order simplification $-r'_A=k'_r C_A$.

Lecture 4-1 · matching units

Writing the flux as a rate per catalyst mass

$-r'_A \;\Longleftrightarrow\; \dfrac{W_A\,A}{m_{cat}} = W_A\, a$ rate per kg uses the specific area $a$
$a=\dfrac{A}{m_{cat}}=\dfrac{\pi d_p^2}{\rho_c\,\pi d_p^3/6}=\dfrac{6}{\rho_c\,d_p}$ sphere: outer area ÷ mass, $[\text{m}^2/\text{kg}]$
$-r'_A = k_c\,a\,(C_{Ab}-C_{As})$ external-MT rate in reaction-rate units
Smaller particles ($d_p\!\downarrow$) give a larger specific area $a$ — more surface per kilogram, so faster external transfer.

Lecture 4-1 · how to get $k_c$

Three dimensionless groups give $k_c$

Sherwood

$\mathrm{Sh}=\dfrac{k_c d_p}{D}=\dfrac{\text{convective MT}}{\text{molecular diffusion}}$

Reynolds

$\mathrm{Re}=\dfrac{\rho\,d_p v}{\mu}=\dfrac{\text{inertial}}{\text{viscous}}$

Schmidt

$\mathrm{Sc}=\dfrac{\mu}{\rho D}=\dfrac{\text{momentum diff.}}{\text{mass diff.}}$

$$\mathrm{Sh}=f(\mathrm{Re},\mathrm{Sc})=A+B\,\mathrm{Re}^{m}\mathrm{Sc}^{n}$$

A general correlation: the constants depend on the geometry and flow regime.

Lecture 4-1 · single sphere

Frössling correlation and how $k_c$ scales

$$\mathrm{Sh}=2+0.6\,\mathrm{Re}^{1/2}\mathrm{Sc}^{1/3}$$
MT-limited ($0.6\,\mathrm{Re}^{1/2}\mathrm{Sc}^{1/3}\gg2$): $\;\dfrac{k_c d_p}{D}\cong0.6\left(\dfrac{d_p\rho u}{\mu}\right)^{1/2}\!\left(\dfrac{\mu}{\rho D}\right)^{1/3}$ drop the "2"
$k_c\;\propto\;D^{2/3}\,\dfrac{\rho^{1/6}}{\mu^{1/6}}\,\dfrac{u^{1/2}}{d_p^{1/2}}$ so $k_c\!\propto\!u^{1/2}$ and $k_c\!\propto\!d_p^{-1/2}$

Faster flow and smaller particles both speed external transfer — the basis of the diagnostic tests in Lecture 4-3.

Lecture 4-2

How do we determine the internal mass-transfer rate (diffusion)?

Once at the surface, A must diffuse through the tortuous pore network to reach active sites inside the pellet.

Lecture 4-2 · internal diffusion

Pore diffusion follows Fick's law

$$W_A=-D_e\dfrac{dC_A}{dr}\quad\left[\dfrac{\text{mol A}}{\text{m}^2\cdot\text{s}}\right]$$
Assume transport only in the radial ($r$) direction inside a spherical pellet.
$D_e$ is the effective diffusivity — pores are narrow, winding, and only partly open.

Lecture 4-2 · magnitudes

Diffusivity depends strongly on the phase

MediumTypical $D_{AB}$ (m²/s)Why
Gas, 1 atm$\sim10^{-5}$molecules far apart, fast
Gas, 10 atm$\sim10^{-6}$denser → more collisions
Liquid$\sim10^{-9}$close-packed molecules
Solid$\sim10^{-12}$ and belowlattice hopping
Gas $D_{AB}\propto T^{3/2}/P$; liquid $D_{AB}\propto T/\mu$. In pores the free-fluid value is only the starting point.

Lecture 4-2 · which diffusivity?

The pore size decides the diffusion regime

Bulk (molecular)

$\ell \ll \delta$: molecule–molecule collisions dominate — ordinary $D_{AB}$.

Knudsen

$\ell \gg \delta$: molecules hit walls more than each other — $D_K\propto d_{pore}\sqrt{T/M}$.

Configurational

Pore ≈ molecule size (zeolites) — diffusivity falls by orders of magnitude.

$\ell=$ mean free path, $\delta=$ pore diameter. Higher pressure or narrower pores move you bulk → Knudsen → configurational, and $D$ drops.

Lecture 4-2 · correcting for real pores

Effective diffusivity $D_e$

$$D_e=\dfrac{D\,\phi_p\,\sigma}{\tau}$$

$\phi_p$ — porosity

Fraction of pellet volume that is open pore.

$\sigma$ — constriction

Corrects for pores that narrow and widen.

$\tau$ — tortuosity

$\dfrac{\text{actual path}}{\text{straight path}}$; winding ⇒ larger $\tau$ ⇒ smaller $D_e$.

All three make $D_e$ smaller than the free-fluid $D$ — often by 5–10×.

Lecture 4-3

What is the rate expression when external mass transfer is the limiting step?

Transport to the surface and reaction happen in series. Combine the resistances, then take the limiting case.

Lecture 4-3 · combining steps

External transfer and reaction act in series

$r'=W_A a = k_c a\,(C_{Ab}-C_{As})$ Step 1 — supply of A
$r'=k'_r C_{As}$ Step 3–5 — first-order surface reaction
$C_{As}=\dfrac{k_c a\,C_{Ab}}{k'_r+k_c a}$ $C_{As}$ can't be measured — eliminate it
$$r'=\dfrac{k'_r\,k_c a\,C_{Ab}}{k'_r+k_c a}$$

At steady state the two rates are equal; the smaller coefficient controls.

Lecture 4-3 · take the limit

Both limits are first order — in different constants

CaseExternal MT limitedChemical-rate limited
Condition$k_c a \ll k'_r$$k_c a \gg k'_r$
Rate expression$r'=k_c a\,C_{Ab}$$r'=k'_r\,C_{Ab}$
Controlled bytransport ($k_c$)kinetics ($k'_r$)
Effect of velocity $u$strong ($k_c\!\propto\!u^{1/2}$)none
Effect of temperatureweakstrong (Arrhenius)

Lecture 4-3 · experimental tests

How do we know external MT is limiting?

Change velocity $u$

External-MT limited: rate rises with $u$, then plateaus. Reaction limited: rate flat.

Change particle size $d_p$

External-MT limited: rate falls as $d_p$ grows ($k_c a\!\propto\!d_p^{-3/2}$). Reaction limited: flat.

$k_c\!\propto\!u^{1/2}$ and $k_c a\!\propto\!d_p^{-3/2}$: doubling $u$ raises $k_c$ by only ≈ 41%.

The next two interactives let you see both curves change live.

Small $k'_r$ → rate is flat (reaction limited). Large $k'_r$ → rate climbs with $u$ (external limited).

Lecture 4-3 · reading the data

Reaction limited vs external-MT limited

Plot the observed rate $r'$ against $u^{1/2}/d_p^{3/2}$ (the group $k_c a$ scales with):

  • Low $k_c a$ (left): external MT limited — rate rises steeply with the group.
  • High $k_c a$ (right): reaction limited — rate plateaus at $k'_r C_{Ab}$.
The knee separates the two regimes — operate to the right of it to remove the transport artefact.
4-4

Lecture 4-4

What is the rate expression when internal mass transfer is the limiting step?

We need the concentration profile of A inside the pellet — it tells us whether diffusion or reaction controls.

Lecture 4-4 · the idea

The profile of A tells us who is slower

How do we know whether internal diffusion or the reaction is the slower step?

We need the concentration profile of A inside the catalyst. From its shape we can tell which rate controls:

  • Flat profile → A reaches the centre → reaction controls.
  • Steep profile → A consumed near the surface → diffusion controls.

Lecture 4-4 · steady-state diffusion

Shell balance on a spherical pellet

$[\text{A in at }r]-[\text{A out at }r+\Delta r]+[\text{generation}]=0$
$\dfrac{d(W_{Ar}r^2)}{dr}-r'\rho_c r^2=0$ divide by $4\pi\Delta r$, let $\Delta r\to0$
$W_A=-D_e\dfrac{dC_A}{dr},\quad r'=k'_{r,n}C_A^{\,n}$ insert Fick's law and the rate law
$$\dfrac{d^2C_A}{dr^2}+\dfrac{2}{r}\dfrac{dC_A}{dr}-\dfrac{\rho_c}{D_e}k'_{r,n}C_A^{\,n}=0$$

Lecture 4-4 · the symbols

Three dimensionless variables

SymbolDefinitionMeaning
$\lambda$$\lambda=\dfrac{r}{R}$dimensionless radius (0 = centre, 1 = surface)
$\psi$$\psi=\dfrac{C_A}{C_{As}}$dimensionless concentration
$\phi_n$ (Thiele)$\phi_n=\sqrt{\dfrac{k'_n\rho_c R^2 C_{As}^{\,n-1}}{D_e}}$reaction rate ÷ diffusion rate
$\eta$ (Weisz)$\eta=\dfrac{r'_{avg}}{r'_s}$effectiveness factor (Lecture 4-5)

Lecture 4-4 · non-dimensionalize

One dimensionless group: the Thiele modulus

$$\dfrac{d^2\psi}{d\lambda^2}+\dfrac{2}{\lambda}\dfrac{d\psi}{d\lambda}-\phi_n^{\,2}\psi^{\,n}=0$$
$\phi_n^{\,2}=\dfrac{k'_{n}\rho_c R^2 C_{As}^{\,n-1}}{D_e}=\dfrac{\text{reaction rate @ surface}}{\text{diffusion rate}}$ the Thiele modulus squared

$\phi_n\gg1\Rightarrow$ internal-diffusion control  ·  $\phi_n\to0\Rightarrow$ surface-reaction control

Boundary conditions: $C_A$ finite at $\lambda=0$ (centre), $C_A=C_{As}$ at $\lambda=1$ (surface).

Lecture 4-4 · analytic solution

Concentration profile for a first-order reaction

$$\psi=\dfrac{C_A}{C_{As}}=\dfrac{1}{\lambda}\,\dfrac{\sinh(\phi_1\lambda)}{\sinh\phi_1}$$

Small $\phi_1$

$\psi\approx1$ everywhere — A fills the whole pellet.

Large $\phi_1$

$\psi$ decays fast — A only near the surface; the core is dead.

See the next interactive: drag $\phi_1$ and watch the profile steepen.

Lecture 4-5

Why do we need the effectiveness factor $\eta$?

How large must $\phi$ be to call it diffusion-limited? A single 0–1 number makes the judgement easy.

Lecture 4-5 · definition

What the effectiveness factor measures

$$\eta=\dfrac{\text{actual overall rate}}{\text{rate at surface conditions}}=\dfrac{r'_{\text{avg}}}{r'_s}=\dfrac{M_A}{r'_s W_c}$$

$\eta\to1$

Interior fully bathed in A — reaction controls, no diffusion penalty.

$\eta\ll1$

A consumed near the surface — most of the pellet is starved.

Then

$r'=\eta\,r'_s$ — one factor rescales the surface-kinetics rate to reality.

Lecture 4-5 · from flux to rate

The overall rate is the flux at the surface

$M_A=r'_{avg}\,W_c=W_{Ar}\big|_{r=R}\times4\pi R^2$ all A entering the pellet reacts
$M_A=4\pi R^2 D_e\dfrac{dC_A}{dr}\Big|_{r=R}=4\pi R D_e C_{As}\dfrac{d\psi}{d\lambda}\Big|_{\lambda=1}$ Fick's law at the surface
$\eta=\dfrac{M_A}{r'_s W_c}=\dfrac{3}{\phi_1^{\,2}}\left(\phi_1\coth\phi_1-1\right)$ use the first-order profile slope

The whole result comes from one quantity — the concentration gradient at the surface.

Lecture 4-5 · first-order sphere

Closed form for a first-order spherical pellet

$$\eta=\dfrac{3}{\phi_1^{\,2}}\big(\phi_1\coth\phi_1-1\big)$$

Small $\phi_1\;(<0.4)$

$\eta\approx1$ — reaction limited. Whole pellet is used.

Large $\phi_1\;(>20)$

$\eta\approx\dfrac{3}{\phi_1}$ — diffusion limited. Only a thin outer shell reacts.

Diffusion-limited rate: $r'=\dfrac{3}{R}\sqrt{\dfrac{D_e k'_1}{\rho_c}}\;C_{Ab}$ — now depends on $\sqrt{k'_1}$ and on $1/R$.

Lecture 4-5 · design levers

What raises $\eta$ (diffusion-limited pellet)

ChangeEffect on $\eta$Why
Pellet size $R\downarrow$↑shorter diffusion path, smaller $\phi$
Diffusivity $D_e\uparrow$↑A penetrates deeper
Rate constant $k'\uparrow$ / $T\uparrow$↓A consumed before it diffuses in
Concentration $C_{As}\uparrow$ ($n>1$)↓faster local consumption
Temperature is subtle: raising $T$ speeds reaction far more than diffusion, so $\eta$ usually falls.

Lecture 4-5 · putting it together

Rate expression in each regime (first order)

Internal-diffusion limitedChemical-rate limited
Condition$\phi_1>20\Rightarrow\eta\approx3/\phi_1$$\phi_1<0.4\Rightarrow\eta\approx1$

Both raise the rate with $T$ and $C$; only the diffusion-limited case improves when you shrink the pellet.

Lecture 4-6

Which step is the controlling mechanism?

The apparent activation energy plus the Weisz–Prater and Mears criteria tell us which resistance dominates.

Lecture 4-6 · overview

Each regime has a different fingerprint

RegimeVelocity $u$Particle $d_p$Temperature ($E_{app}$)
External MTstrong ↑strong ↓weak (<10 kJ/mol)
Internal diffusionnonemoderate ↓$\approx E/2$
Reactionnonenonefull $E$ (>50 kJ/mol)
Use these three levers experimentally to pin down which step controls, then pick the right rate expression.

Lecture 4-6 · temperature fingerprint

The Arrhenius slope reveals the regime

$$k(T)=A\,e^{-E_a/RT}\;\Rightarrow\;\ln(\text{rate})\text{ vs }1/T\text{ gives } E_{app}$$
  • Reaction limited: slope $=-E/R$, $\;E_{app}>50$ kJ/mol
  • Pore-diffusion limited: slope $=-E/2R$, $\;E_{app}\approx\tfrac12 E$
  • Bulk-diffusion limited: slope shallow, $\;E_{app}<10$ kJ/mol

Lecture 4-6 · internal diffusion test

Weisz–Prater criterion (internal)

$$C_{WP}=\eta\,\phi_1^{\,2}=\dfrac{r'_s(\text{obs})\,\rho_c R^2}{D_e\,C_{As}}$$

$C_{WP}\ll1$

e.g. $0.0001$ — no internal-diffusion limit. Reaction controls.

$C_{WP}\gg1$

e.g. $50$ — severe internal-diffusion limitation.

All observable

Uses the measured rate, known $\rho_c,R$, and an estimated $D_e$.

Interactive 14 — size the diffusion effect

Weisz–Prater calculator

Weisz–Prater number $C_{WP}$

$C_{As}=1\ \text{mol/m}^3$ · $C_{WP}=\dfrac{(r'_s\rho_c)R^2}{D_e C_{As}}$

Lecture 4-6 · external diffusion test

Mears' criterion (external)

$$\dfrac{-r'_A\,\rho_c\,R\,n}{k_c\,C_{Ab}}<0.15$$

If satisfied, external mass transfer is not limiting, so $C_{As}=C_{Ab}$.

$n$ = order, $R$ = radius, $\rho_c$ = catalyst density, $k_c$ = MT coefficient, $C_{Ab}$ = bulk concentration.
For most catalytic reactions, internal diffusion matters more than external — check Weisz–Prater first.

Class Activity · Problem 1

Problem 1 — is there a mass-transfer effect?

Conversion is plotted against $W/F$ for two particle sizes (a) and (b). In case (a) the two curves coincide; in case (b) the high-velocity curve lies above the low-velocity one. Which case shows a mass-transfer effect?
(a) coincide → no external MT effect. If changing particle size / velocity does not move the conversion curve, transport is not limiting — the intrinsic kinetics control.
(b) curves split by velocity → external MT effect present. Higher velocity raises $k_c$ and hence conversion, the signature of external-diffusion limitation.

Class Activity · Problem 2

Problem 2 — Dowden & Bridger SO₂ → SO₃

Oxidation of SO₂ was studied at 400 and 470 °C with two pellet sizes (2.36 & 1.14 mm) and varying mass velocity. Conversion rises with mass velocity and with smaller pellets. Interpret.
Sensitivity to mass velocity ⇒ external mass transfer is limiting (more velocity → larger $k_c$).
Sensitivity to pellet size at fixed velocity ⇒ internal diffusion also matters (smaller $R$ → smaller $\phi$ → larger $\eta$).
Conclusion: the data are transport-controlled, not intrinsic-kinetics-controlled — both external and internal diffusion contribute.

Class Activity · Problem 3

Problem 3 — first-order pellet, find the rate at 448 K

A first-order reaction A→B runs in an $R=0.20$ cm sphere, $D_e=0.015$ cm²/s. At 398 K the observed rate is $2.63\times10^{-5}$ mol/(cm³·s) with $C_A=3.25\times10^{-5}$ mol/cm³ and $E=20$ kcal/mol. Find the rate at 448 K.
Arrhenius: $k_{448}=k_{398}\exp\!\left[\tfrac{E}{R}\!\left(\tfrac1{398}-\tfrac1{448}\right)\right]$ raises $k'$ from ≈ 0.81 to ≈ 13.6 s⁻¹ (≈ 17×).
Higher $k'$ raises $\phi_1=\sqrt{k'_1\rho_c R^2/D_e}$, so $\eta$ drops — recompute $\eta=\tfrac{3}{\phi_1^2}(\phi_1\coth\phi_1-1)$ at 448 K.
Answer: $r_{448}\approx4.43\times10^{-4}$ mol/(cm³·s) — about 17× the 398 K rate, tempered by the falling $\eta$.

Class Activity · Problem 4

Problem 4 — read the rate-vs-flow curves

A differential packed bed is run at several temperatures, flow rates, and particle sizes. Which conditions are external-MT limited, reaction limited, and internal-diffusion controlled?
External MT: rate still rising with flow rate at fixed $T$ — not yet on the plateau.
Reaction limited: rate independent of flow rate and of particle size, strong $T$ dependence (full $E$).
Internal diffusion: rate independent of flow but rises when particles are made smaller; $E_{app}\approx E/2$.

Class Activity · Problem 6

Problem 6 — effectiveness factor & Weisz–Prater

Ethyl-acetate synthesis on a 3.5 mm spherical resin, first order in ethanol, pseudo-zero-order in acetic acid, $\rho_c=2000$ kg/m³, bed void 0.50, $D_e\approx2\times10^{-8}$ m²/s. Find the effectiveness factor and decide (Weisz–Prater) whether pore diffusion matters.
Step 1 — Weisz–Prater: $C_{WP}=\dfrac{r'_s\rho_c R^2}{D_e C_{As}}$ from the measured rate. If $C_{WP}\gtrsim1$, pore diffusion is important.
Step 2 — back out $\phi_1$ from $C_{WP}=\eta\phi_1^2$, then $\eta=\dfrac{3}{\phi_1^2}(\phi_1\coth\phi_1-1)$.
Use Interactive 14 (Weisz–Prater) and Interactive 10 (Thiele) to get $C_{WP}$ and $\eta$ for these numbers.

Problem 7 — sketch A, B, C inside the pellet

Gas reaction $\mathrm{A}+2\mathrm{B}\to3\mathrm{C}$ on a spherical catalyst, $d_p=0.4$ mm, film ≈ 0.1 mm. Sketch $C_A,C_B,C_C$ from the centre to the bulk when (a) external MT ≫ internal ≫ reaction, and (b) reaction ≫ external MT.

Key Takeaways

1

Transport and reaction act in series: $r'=\dfrac{k'_r k_c a}{k'_r+k_c a}C_{Ab}$ — the smaller coefficient controls.

2

The Thiele modulus $\phi$ compares reaction to diffusion; $\eta=\tfrac{3}{\phi^2}(\phi\coth\phi-1)$ rescales the rate.

3

Diagnose the regime: velocity & particle-size tests, Mears (external), Weisz–Prater (internal), apparent $E_{app}$.

4

To beat diffusion limits, shrink the pellet ($R\downarrow$) — it lowers $\phi$ and lifts $\eta$.
