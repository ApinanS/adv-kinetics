The big picture

How do we determine a rate expression?

Kinetic parameter estimation — the recipe

Eight moves from experiment to constants

  • Experiment to see which variables affect the reaction.
  • Fix the mechanism / pathway / steps.
  • If multiphase: find conditions with no physical-rate effect (mass & heat transfer).
  • Choose the rate-limiting step of the chemical rate.
  • Derive the rate expression from that mechanism.
  • Collect data in a well-chosen experimental reactor.
  • Regress data vs rate law → consistent? extract parameters; else loop back.
Isothermal operation removes the energy balance, so every reactor here is run at constant $T$.
"Apparent rate" = the rate you measure before stripping out transport effects. Sections 7-8 & 7-10 strip them out.

Which type of lab-scale reactor should we use to determine the rate expression?

BatchMixed flowIntegral tubularDifferential tubular

Experimental reactors · isothermal

Four families of kinetic reactor

1 · Batch

Fluid (& catalyst basket) charged once; sample $C_j(t)$. First choice for kinetics.

2 · Mixed flow (CSTR)

Gradientless / internal-recycle (Berty, Carberry). Steady state at one uniform $C_j$.

3 · Integral tubular

Fixed bed; large conversion along the bed. Mimics the commercial reactor.

4 · Differential tubular

Thin bed; tiny conversion so $C_j\approx$ const → read $-r_j$ directly.

Family 1 · batch reactor

Batch: the kineticist’s first choice

When: first kinetic study — easy to eliminate momentum, heat & mass-transfer effects and to hold $T$.
Limits: sample vs total volume, heat-up/cool-down lag, cannot watch deactivation, poor for gas phase, does not mimic a fixed bed.

Family 2 · mixed-flow / gradientless

Mixed flow: one uniform concentration

$$\tau=\frac{V}{v_0}=\frac{V}{F_0}\qquad -r_j=\frac{F_{j0}-F_j}{W}$$
Run at constant concentration (steady state). Vary residence time by changing flow — without disturbing heat/mass transfer.
Limits: time to reach steady state; residence-time distribution; cannot observe deactivation.

Families 3 & 4 · tubular flow

Integral vs differential tubular reactor

Conversion across bedlarge, $X$ grows with $W/F_0$small, $\Delta C$ tiny
What you read$F_j$ vs $W/F_{0}$ (integrate)$-r_j$ vs $C_j$ directly
Best forvalidating a model; heat/mass-transfer & deactivationrate that depends strongly on $C_j$

How do we determine the parameters for a homogeneous multiple (series) reaction?

Batch reactorDifferential method$A\to B\to C$

Isothermal batch · series reaction

Three coupled mole balances

$$A\xrightarrow{\,k_1\,}B\xrightarrow{\,k_2\,}C$$
$\dfrac{dC_A}{dt}=-k_1C_A$A only disappears
$\dfrac{dC_B}{dt}=k_1C_A-k_2C_B$B formed then consumed
$\dfrac{dC_C}{dt}=k_2C_B$C accumulates
Initial: $C_A=1,\;C_B=C_C=0$. No analytical shortcut for two unknowns → use the differential (numerical) method.
$C_B$ passes through a maximum — the fingerprint of a series reaction.

Code ↔ equations · two unknowns $k_1,k_2$

How do we get the parameters for a solid-catalysed multiple reaction?

Batch basket reactorDifferential methodApparent rate

Worked system · glycerol carbonate production

A reversible step plus a side reaction

$$\text{GLY}+\text{DMC}\underset{k_2}{\overset{k_1}{\rightleftharpoons}}\text{GLC}+2\,\text{Me}\qquad\qquad \text{GLC}\xrightarrow{\,k_3\,}\text{GLD}+\text{CO}_2$$
$r_1=k_1C_{GLY}C_{DMC}-k_2C_{GLC}C_{Me}^2$main, reversible
$r_2=k_3C_{GLC}$side: GLC→glycidol
GLY = glycerol, DMC = dimethyl carbonate, GLC = glycerol carbonate, Me = methanol, GLD = glycidol. Basket reactor: $V=300$ mL, $T=75\,^\circ$C, $M_{cat}=0.84$ g.

Why divide by catalyst mass?

Mole balances on an “apparent” rate

$\dfrac{1}{M_{cat}}\dfrac{dN_{GLY}}{dt}=-r_1$reactant
$\dfrac{1}{M_{cat}}\dfrac{dN_{GLC}}{dt}=r_1-r_2$intermediate
$\dfrac{1}{M_{cat}}\dfrac{dN_{Me}}{dt}=2\,r_1$2 per main step
$\dfrac{1}{M_{cat}}\dfrac{dN_{GLD}}{dt}=r_2$side product
Rate is normalised per gram of catalyst — that is the meaning of $-r'_j$ for a solid-catalysed reaction.
It is apparent because transport effects are not yet removed. We test that in 7-8 (external) and 7-10 (internal).
Concentrations close the system: $C_j=N_j/V$.

Reading the differential-reactor data

Let the data tell you the form

  • Low $P_A$: $-r'_A\propto P_A$ → A adsorbs on a site.
  • High $P_A$: $-r'_A$ becomes independent of $P_A$ → the surface saturates.
  • At all pressures $-r'_A\propto 1/P_B$ → B sits in the denominator (adsorbed, inhibiting).

From clues to a single-site LH law

The Langmuir–Hinshelwood form

$$-r'_A=\frac{k\,P_A}{1+K_A P_A+K_B P_B}$$
$P_A\!\to\!0:\;-r'_A\approx kP_A/(1{+}K_BP_B)$ → linear, as observed.
$P_A\!\to\!\infty:\;-r'_A\to k/K_A$ → the saturation plateau.
Fitting both data sets gives $k\approx1.0,\;K_A\approx0.52,\;K_B\approx8.1$ — B adsorbs $\sim$16× more strongly than A.

Is there an external mass-transfer limitation in the measured rate?

Basket reactorMears’ criterionApparent rate

Diagnose before you trust the rate

The stirring test and Mears’ criterion

$$\frac{-r'_A\,\rho_b\,R\,n}{k_c\,C_{Ab}}<0.15$$
If the inequality holds, the film resistance is negligible: $C_{As}\approx C_{Ab}$.
  • Vary stirring speed / flow. If the rate keeps rising, the film still limits.
  • When the rate stops changing with agitation → you are in the kinetic regime.
  • $r'$ = observed rate, $n$ = order, $R$ = particle radius, $\rho_b$ = bed density, $k_c$ = film coefficient.

Estimating the film coefficient

Where $k_c$ comes from

Sherwood

$Sh=\dfrac{k_c d_p}{D_{AB}}=2+0.6\,Re^{1/2}Sc^{1/3}$

stirred / two-phase correlations (Vasquez & Bautista, 1997).

Schmidt

$Sc=\dfrac{\mu}{\rho D_{AB}}$

links momentum and mass diffusion.

Diffusivity

$D_{AB}\propto\dfrac{T}{\mu}$ (Wilke–Chang)

liquid-phase estimate for $D_{AB}$.

How do we get the effectiveness factor and effective diffusivity?

Gas-phase differential reactorThiele modulusWeisz–Prater

Internal diffusion in a pellet

Thiele modulus → effectiveness factor

$$\phi=\frac{d_p}{2}\sqrt{\frac{k}{D_e}}\qquad \eta=\frac{3}{\phi^2}\big(\phi\coth\phi-1\big)$$
$$r_{obs}=\eta\,k\,C_{As}\qquad C_{WP}=\eta\phi^2=\frac{r_{obs}R^2}{D_e C_{As}}$$
$\phi\ll1$: $\eta\to1$, whole pellet used. $\phi\gg1$: $\eta\to3/\phi$, only the shell reacts.
Weisz–Prater: $C_{WP}\ll1$ → no internal limitation; $C_{WP}\gg1$ → pore-diffusion controlled.

Compensating for decay

Raise $T$ to keep conversion constant

$$k(T)\,a(t)=\text{const}\;\Rightarrow\;\frac{1}{T}=\frac{1}{T_0}-\frac{R}{E_a}\ln\!\big(1+k_d t\big)$$
As $a$ drops, $k$ must rise by the same factor — so the set-point temperature creeps upward over the run.
The trajectory ends when $T$ hits a metallurgical or runaway-sintering limit: that fixes the catalyst lifetime.
