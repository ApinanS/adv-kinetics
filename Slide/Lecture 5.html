What is a heterogeneous phase reaction?

Lecture 5-1 · Definition

Reaction happens in one phase — reactants must meet first

No matter how many phases are present, the reaction mostly takes place in only one phase
If the reactants sit in different phases, one must dissolve into the other phase before the reaction can occur
Consequence: the overall rate is a series of mass transfer and chemical reaction

Lecture 5-1 · Real examples

Multiphase systems found in industry

Transesterification (L–L / L–L–S)

Triglyceride + Methanol → Biodiesel + Glycerol, with homogeneous (NaOH) or heterogeneous (solid) catalyst

Hydrogenation (G–L)

H₂ must dissolve into the liquid before reacting — also oxidation, chlorination, alkoxylation

Hydrotreating / HDO (G–L–S)

Hydrodeoxygenation of plant oil: H₂ (gas) + oil (liquid) + solid catalyst

Fermentation (G–L–S)

O₂ dissolves into the liquid broth, then cells/enzymes catalyze the reaction — bioreaction

Lecture 5-2

What is the rate expression for a 2-phase L–L or G–L reaction? (non-elementary)

Lecture 5-2 · Setup

$A(\text{dispersed}) + B(\text{continuous}) \rightarrow$ Product

A is in the dispersed phase (droplets), B is in the continuous phase
Here the reaction occurs in the continuous phase — A must first cross the interface
$$C_{Aci}=K_{cd}\,C_{Adi},\qquad K_{cd}<1$$

Lecture 5-2 · Mechanism

The rate arises from 3 steps in series

1. Transport (dispersed → interface): $\;r_{md}=k_{md}\,a\,(C_{Adb}-C_{Adi})$
dispersed-side film
2. Transport (interface → continuous): $\;r_{mc}=k_{mc}\,a\,(C_{Aci}-C_{Acb})$
continuous-side film
3. Reaction in continuous phase: $\;r_1=k_r\,C_{Bcb}\,C_{Acb}$
chemical reaction
$a=\dfrac{\text{mass transfer area}}{\text{reaction volume}}=\dfrac{\text{surface of dispersed particle}}{\text{reaction volume}}$

Lecture 5-2 · Combine (steady state)

Overall rate = driving force ÷ sum of resistances

At steady state: $\;r_1=r_{md}=r_{mc}=r$
$$r=\cfrac{C_{Adb}}{\dfrac{1}{k_r K_{cd} C_{Bcb}}+\dfrac{1}{k_{md}a}+\dfrac{1}{K_{cd}k_{mc}a}}$$

Three terms in the denominator = resistances of reaction · dispersed film · continuous film

Lecture 5-2 · Interactive — concentration profile

Which step is the “bottleneck” that controls the rate?

Red dot = $C_{Adb}$ · green dot = $C_{Acb}$ · the steepest drop is the bottleneck

Lecture 5-2 · Interactive — Chart

Raise stirring / interfacial area $a$ and the rate saturates

Dashed line = ceiling when reaction-controlled: $r_{max}=k_r K_{cd} C_{Bcb}C_{Adb}$

Lecture 5-2 · Interactive — Arrhenius

Why mass-transfer control is less sensitive to temperature

$\dfrac{1}{k_{app}}=\dfrac{1}{k_{rxn}(T)}+\dfrac{1}{k_{m}a}$
Axes: $\ln k_{app}$ vs $1000/T$ — steep = reaction control (T-sensitive), flat = mass-transfer control

Lecture 5-3

What is the rate expression for a 3-phase G–L–S or L–L–S reaction? (non-elementary)

Lecture 5-3 · Setup

$A(g) + B(l) \xrightarrow{\text{solid catalyst}}$ Product

A diffuses from the gas → through the G/L interface → into the liquid → to the catalyst surface → active site
$C_{Agi}=K_{gi}\,C_{Ali}$ — Henry's constant at the gas–liquid surface
Five steps in series: gas film · G–L · liquid film · L–S film · reaction

Lecture 5-3 · Notation

Interfacial areas and phase fractions

$a_i=\dfrac{\text{gas–liquid interfacial area}}{\text{volume of reactor}}$
G–L area per reactor volume
$a_c=\dfrac{\text{external surface of particle}}{\text{volume of particle}}$
particle surface area
$f_s=\dfrac{\text{volume of particles}}{\text{volume of reactor}}$
solid loading
$f_l=\dfrac{\text{volume of liquid}}{\text{volume of reactor}}$
liquid loading

Lecture 5-3 · Mechanism

Five steps in series (A limiting)

1. Gas film:  $r_1'=\dfrac{k_g a_i}{(1-\varepsilon)\rho_p}(C_{Agb}-C_{Agi})$
bulk gas → interface
2. Liquid film:  $r_2'=\dfrac{k_l a_i}{(1-\varepsilon)\rho_p}(C_{Ali}-C_{Alb})$
interface → bulk liquid
3. Liquid–solid:  $r_3'=k_c a_c(C_{Alb}-C_{As})$
bulk liquid → surface
4–5. Reaction:  $r_{45}'=\dfrac{\eta\,k_r' C_{As} C_{Bs}}{1+K_A C_{As}+K_B C_{Bs}}$
ads + rxn + desorption ($\eta$ = effectiveness)

Lecture 5-3 · Interactive — Concentration cascade

Track A from the gas to the active site

The steepest concentration drop is the rate-limiting step

Lecture 5-3 · Combine

Overall rate when A is the limiting reactant

$$r'=\cfrac{C_{Agb}}{\dfrac{K_{gL}}{\eta k_r' C_{Bs}}+\dfrac{(1-\varepsilon)\rho_p}{k_g a_i}+\dfrac{K_{gL}(1-\varepsilon)\rho_p}{k_l a_i}+\dfrac{K_{gL}}{k_c a_c}}$$

Four terms = resistances of reaction · gas film · liquid film · liquid–solid film

If $C_B\gg C_A$ ($C_{Bs}=C_{Bb}$): $\;r'=k_{app}'\,C_{Agb}$ (pseudo-first order)

Lecture 5-3 · Apparent rate

Different limiting reactant → different apparent rate

CaseA limiting ($C_B\gg C_A$)B limiting ($C_A\gg C_B$)
Assumption$C_{Bs}=C_{Bb}$$C_{As}=C_{Ab}$
Apparent rate$r'=k_{app}'\,C_{Agb}$$r'=k_{app}'\,C_{Blb}$
Order1st order in $C_{Agb}$1st order in $C_{Blb}$

Lecture 5-3 · Remark

The physical rate changes with scale

Unlike the chemical steps (adsorption, surface reaction, desorption), the physical (mass-transfer) rate changes with scale and operating parameters
Result: the apparent rate can differ at each scale — the controlling step may change

Lecture 5-4

Which rate expression do we use to design an industrial reactor?

Lecture 5-4 · Practice

The rate expression comes from experimental data

  • The rate expression is obtained from experimental data
  • To design a commercial-scale reactor, validate it against results from several sizes: lab → pilot 1 → pilot 2
The closer to full scale, the more confident the rate expression truly holds

Lecture 5-4 · Key insight

Chemical rate is constant, but the physical rate changes

  • The rate expression may change with scale
  • Chemical rate does not change but the physical rate changes with scale → a different limiting step at each scale
  • So the exact rate expression is not used for design, even for a heterogeneous phase

Lecture 5-4 · Interactive — Scale-up

Scale the reactor up and the controlling step shifts

As scale increases: $k_l a_i,\,k_g a_i$ fall (harder to mix) → apparent rate drops and shifts to mass-transfer control

Lecture 5-4 · During design

Use the apparent rate + power law

  • Use the apparent rate expression to design reactors of various sizes
  • The rate is a function of the limiting-reactant concentration
  • Usually a simple power law — first-order reaction
$$r=k_{app}\,C_{Ab}$$

Key Takeaways

1

A heterogeneous reaction occurs in one phase — reactants must dissolve across phases first

2

Overall rate = driving force ÷ sum of series resistances (mass transfer + reaction)

3

Mass-transfer control → sensitive to $a$/stirring, low $E_a$ · Reaction control → depends on $C_B$, high $E_a$

4

Design with an apparent rate (power law) validated across scales
