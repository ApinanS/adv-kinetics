Building the reaction rate expression

Every reactor design begins with one question: how fast? This lesson walks the three-lecture arc that answers it — from defining what a reaction rate even is (L1), to writing rate laws for elementary and biological reactions (L2), to the Langmuir–Hinshelwood machinery for solid-catalytic reactions where the chemistry, not transport, sets the pace (L3). Part 1Reaction Kinetics FundamentalsLecture 1 — what a rate is, and how temperature drives it
1.1

Reaction rate is the consuming rate of the limiting reactant — made scale-free

One reaction has one rate; every species' rate is that rate times its stoichiometric coefficient.

For a single reaction \(a\mathrm{A}+b\mathrm{B}\rightarrow c\mathrm{C}+d\mathrm{D}\), the raw "how many moles of A disappear per second" depends on how big your reactor is. To get an intrinsic property we normalise by the volume of reaction (homogeneous), the mass of catalyst \(W\) (solid-catalytic), or the catalyst surface area. That normalised number is the rate, and by convention the limiting reactant's stoichiometric coefficient is set to 1.

$$ \frac{-r_\mathrm{A}}{a}=\frac{-r_\mathrm{B}}{b}=\frac{r_\mathrm{C}}{c}=\frac{r_\mathrm{D}}{d}=r_1 $$

Pick the coefficients and the reaction rate \(r_1\), and switch the basis, to see how each species' molar rate follows:

The four bars always stay locked in the ratio a : b : c : d. Change the basis and the number's meaning changes — the physics of the ratio does not.

Instructor's framing. Whether you write the rate per unit volume, per gram of catalyst, or per unit area is a modelling choice — pick the one that makes the mass balance clean. Homogeneous single-phase → volume; solid catalytic → mass of catalyst.
1.2

Arrhenius: temperature's exponential grip on the rate constant

Raise T and \(k\) climbs exponentially; the slope of \(\ln k\) vs \(1/T\) is exactly \(-E_a/R\).

Concentration dependence lives in the rate law; temperature dependence lives in the constant \(k(T)\). Arrhenius gives it as a frequency factor \(A\) throttled by an activation-energy barrier:

$$ k(T)=A\,e^{-E_a/RT}\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad k(T)=k(T_0)\,\exp\!\left[\frac{E_a}{R}\!\left(\frac{1}{T_0}-\frac{1}{T}\right)\right] $$

The two-point form on the right is what you actually use in problems: anchor \(k\) at one temperature \(T_0\), then predict any other \(T\). Drag \(E_a\) and \(T\) and watch both the rate constant and the straight-line Arrhenius plot respond.

Common mistake. \(T\) in Arrhenius is absolute temperature (Kelvin), never °C. Forgetting to convert is the single most frequent error on this topic — and \(1/T\) makes it silently wrong, not obviously wrong.
Beyond the slides. A rule of thumb worth carrying: near room temperature many reactions roughly double their rate for every +10 K. Set \(E_a\approx 50\) kJ/mol and step T from 300→310 K to see how close that "×2 per 10°" folklore really is.
1.3

With side reactions, conversion is not enough — you chase selectivity

When A can become desired C or wasteful U, the concentration you run at tilts the split.

Real processes rarely give one clean reaction. Define conversion \(X=(N_{\mathrm{A}0}-N_\mathrm{A})/N_{\mathrm{A}0}\), and for the desired product C the instantaneous selectivity \(S_{\mathrm{C/U}}=r_\mathrm{C}/r_\mathrm{U}\) and yield. Suppose two parallel paths compete:

$$ \mathrm{A}\xrightarrow{\;k_\mathrm{D}\;}\mathrm{C\ (desired)},\quad r_\mathrm{C}=k_\mathrm{D}C_\mathrm{A}^{\,\alpha_1}\qquad\qquad \mathrm{A}\xrightarrow{\;k_\mathrm{U}\;}\mathrm{U\ (waste)},\quad r_\mathrm{U}=k_\mathrm{U}C_\mathrm{A}^{\,\alpha_2} $$
$$ S_{\mathrm{C/U}}=\frac{r_\mathrm{C}}{r_\mathrm{U}}=\frac{k_\mathrm{D}}{k_\mathrm{U}}\,C_\mathrm{A}^{\,\alpha_1-\alpha_2} $$

The exponent difference \(\alpha_1-\alpha_2\) decides everything. If the desired path is higher-order (\(\alpha_1>\alpha_2\)), selectivity rises with concentration — run concentrated. If it is lower-order, run dilute. Test it:

Part 2Rate Laws for Elementary & Bio ReactionsLecture 2 — from collisions to enzymes and cells
2.1

For an elementary step, the orders are the stoichiometric coefficients

Elementary means single-step, collision-driven — so the rate law reads straight off the equation.

An elementary reaction happens in one step, from the direct collision of the reactants. Only then are you allowed to write the rate as a power law using the stoichiometric coefficients as exponents:

$$ r_1=k_r\,C_\mathrm{A}^{\,a}\,C_\mathrm{B}^{\,b},\qquad \text{order}=a+b $$

The order is the whole personality of the curve. Zero order is a flat line (rate ignores concentration); first order is a straight ramp; second order curves upward. Sweep the order in A and watch the shape morph — the ghosted lines show orders 0, 1, 2 for reference.

Instructor's caution ("ki เท่ากันหรือไม่?"). You may only equate orders to stoichiometric coefficients for a genuinely elementary step. For an overall (non-elementary) reaction the exponents must come from data or a derived mechanism — they need not even be integers.
2.2

Non-elementary reactions: build the rate from a mechanism using RLS + PSSH

Two tools turn a multi-step mechanism into one measurable rate law: the rate-limiting step and pseudo-steady state.

Most real reactions are a sequence of elementary steps. The apparent rate is assembled from them with two moves: (1) pick the rate-limiting step — the slowest link; any reversible non-limiting step is set to equilibrium (\(r=0\)); (2) apply the Pseudo-Steady-State Hypothesis (PSSH) to unstable intermediates (radicals, surface species), whose net rate of formation is taken as zero. The final expression must contain only measurable concentrations. Click through the ethane-pyrolysis derivation:

  1. Propose the mechanism — ethane cracks to ethylene via radical steps.

    \(\mathrm{C_2H_6}\rightarrow\mathrm{C_2H_4}+\mathrm{H_2}\), through initiation, propagation and termination of radicals.

    → this fixes which species are stable (measurable) and which are fleeting intermediates.

  2. Apply PSSH to the radicals — set their net formation rate to zero.

    The radical concentrations are tiny and constant, so \(r_{\text{radical}}\approx 0\) lets you solve for them algebraically.

    → eliminates the unmeasurable \([\text{H}\!\cdot],[\text{C}_2\text{H}_5\!\cdot]\) from the equations.

  3. Insert the rate-limiting step — the slow step carries the overall rate.

    Fast, reversible steps are put at equilibrium (\(K_i=k_i/k_{-i}\)); only the slow step's constant survives out front.

    → collapses many constants into one apparent \(k\).

  4. Result: a rate in measurable concentrations.

    The algebra returns an apparent order (here ≈ 1 in ethane) — a number you can now check against experiment.

    → ready to drop into a reactor mass balance.

When PSSH vs single RLS? Use a single rate-limiting step when one step is clearly slowest. Use PSSH when several competing steps have comparable rates, so no single one dominates — the assumption then pins the reactive intermediates instead.
2.3

Michaelis–Menten: enzyme rate that saturates, and KM is where it's half-full

An enzyme has finite active sites, so rate rises, bends, and plateaus at Vmax; KM marks the half-maximum.

Treating the enzyme–substrate complex as a PSSH intermediate (adsorption then reaction) gives the Michaelis–Menten law — the deck's exact form:

$$ r_P=k_3\frac{C_{E_T}C_S}{K_M+C_S}=\frac{V_{max}\,C_S}{K_M+C_S},\qquad V_{max}=k_3 C_{E_T} $$

At \(C_S=K_M\) the rate is exactly \(V_{max}/2\) — that is the definition of \(K_M\), and the fastest way to read it off a graph. Because the equation is nonlinear, experimentalists often flip it into the straight Lineweaver–Burk double-reciprocal form to fit \(V_{max}\) and \(K_M\):

$$ \frac{1}{r_P}=\frac{K_M}{V_{max}}\frac{1}{C_S}+\frac{1}{V_{max}} $$

2.4

Monod cell growth: the same saturation shape, a biological meaning

Microbial growth obeys Monod — mathematically identical to Michaelis–Menten, with cells eating substrate.

With ample nutrient, cells multiply and the specific growth rate follows Monod kinetics; the growth rate is that specific rate times the cell concentration:

$$ \mu=\mu_{max}\frac{C_S}{K_S+C_S},\qquad r_g=\mu\,C_C=\mu_{max}\frac{C_C\,C_S}{K_S+C_S} $$

Notice the structural twin of Michaelis–Menten: \(\mu_{max}\leftrightarrow V_{max}\), \(K_S\leftrightarrow K_M\). Defaults below are the deck's own culture parameters (\(\mu_{max}=0.7\ \mathrm{h^{-1}}\), \(K_S=10\ \mathrm{mg/dm^3}\)). At \(C_S=K_S\), \(\mu=\mu_{max}/2\).

2.5

Substrate inhibition: too much food poisons the rate (Andrews / Haldane)

Add a \(C_S^2/K_I\) term and the ever-rising Monod curve turns over into a peak.

Some cultures grow slower when substrate is too abundant. The Andrews (Haldane) model captures it with one extra term in the denominator:

$$ \mu=\mu_{max}\frac{C_S}{K_S+C_S+\dfrac{C_S^{2}}{K_I}} $$

That quadratic term is negligible at low \(C_S\) (you recover Monod) but dominates at high \(C_S\), pulling the rate back down. The maximum sits at \(C_S=\sqrt{K_S K_I}\). Toggle Monod vs Andrews and shrink \(K_I\) to make the inhibition bite harder:

Beyond the slides. The same inhibition-by-excess shape governs product inhibition in fermenters and enzyme reactors near saturation. Whenever a measured rate rises then falls with the very species that feeds it, suspect an inhibition term — a cue you'll reuse in L3 with the MEK example.
Part 3Solid Catalytic Reactions — Chemical-Rate ControlledLecture 3 — Langmuir–Hinshelwood–Hougen–Watson kinetics
3.1

A catalytic reaction is seven steps in series — chemistry is only three of them

"Chemical-rate controlled" means the transport steps are fast, so a chemical step is the bottleneck.

For \(\mathrm{A}\rightarrow\mathrm{B}\) on a solid catalyst, a molecule must run a seven-step gauntlet: diffuse from the bulk to the surface, diffuse into the pores, adsorb, react on the surface, desorb, diffuse back out of the pores, and diffuse back to the bulk. Three are chemical-rate steps (adsorption, surface reaction, desorption); four are physical-rate transport steps. Since they act in series, the slowest one controls. Click a step to see its role:

The seven-step sequence — click a step
selected stepTap any block above. External & internal diffusion are physical (transport); adsorption, surface reaction and desorption are the chemical steps.

When the physical (transport) steps are much faster than the chemical steps, concentration at the surface ≈ concentration in the bulk (CAb ≈ CAs ≈ CA), and the rate law is written purely from the chemical steps. That is the "chemical-rate controlled" regime of this lecture.

Why are adsorption and desorption "chemical"? Because the reactant chemisorbs — it forms an actual chemical bond to the active site (e.g. ethylidyne on platinum). Bond formation and breaking, not mere diffusion, is what makes these rate steps chemical.
3.2

The three building-block rates, and the site balance that closes them

Each chemical step is a reversible mass-action rate on sites; a site balance ties the surface coverages together.

Write every chemical step from mass action, counting vacant sites \(S\) as reactants. These are the deck's exact forms. Adsorption of A:

$$ \mathrm{A}+S\rightleftharpoons \mathrm{A\!\cdot\!S}:\quad r_{ad}'=k_a'\!\left(C_\mathrm{A}C_S-\frac{C_{\mathrm{A}\cdot S}}{K_\mathrm{A}}\right) $$

Surface reaction (single-site and dual-site variants) and desorption follow the same pattern:

$$ r_S'=k_S'\!\left(C_{\mathrm{A}\cdot S}-\frac{C_{\mathrm{B}\cdot S}}{K_S}\right)\quad(\text{single site}),\qquad r_S'=k_S'\!\left(C_{\mathrm{A}\cdot S}C_S-\frac{C_{\mathrm{B}\cdot S}C_S}{K_S}\right)\quad(\text{dual site}) $$

The key that unlocks the algebra is the site balance: total sites = vacant + every occupied kind.

$$ C_t=C_S+\sum_i C_{i\cdot S} $$
Mechanism menu. The surface step can be single-site (A·S isomerises), dual-site (A·S reacts with an adjacent site or B·S), or Eley–Rideal (adsorbed A·S reacts with gas-phase B). Which one you assume changes the final rate law — the next section makes that consequence visible.
Beyond the slides. Counting sites is bookkeeping with a physical punchline: every species that sits on a site during the reaction earns a term in the denominator of the final rate law, and the number of distinct sites involved becomes the denominator's exponent. Hold that thought for §3.4.
3.3

The same mechanism, different limiting step → different rate law (Cumene)

Choosing which chemical step is slow rewrites the rate expression — and reshapes its pressure dependence.

Cumene decomposes to benzene and propylene, \(\mathrm{C}\rightarrow\mathrm{B}+\mathrm{P}\), through adsorption → surface reaction → desorption. Assume one step is limiting and set the others to equilibrium; the surviving expression depends entirely on your choice. Toggle the limiting step:

3.4

The LHHW form, and reading K's straight out of differential-reactor data

Rate = (kinetic factor)·(driving force) / (adsorption term)ⁿ — and the data hands you the constants.

The Langmuir–Hinshelwood / Hougen–Watson rate law, for surface reaction limiting, has a fixed anatomy:

$$ -r_\mathrm{A}'=\frac{k_r'\,C_\mathrm{A}^{a}C_\mathrm{B}^{b}}{\big(1+K_\mathrm{A}C_\mathrm{A}+K_\mathrm{B}C_\mathrm{B}+\cdots\big)^{n}} $$

A species appears in the denominator only if it occupies a site; the exponent \(n\) is the number of sites in the limiting step. 3.5

Worked example: MEK from butanol — a rate that peaks means a dual site

A rate that rises then falls with reactant pressure is the signature of a dual-site surface reaction.

Methyl ethyl ketone (MEK) is made by dehydrogenating butan-2-ol (Bu) over ZnO. In a differential reactor at 490 °C the rate rises then falls as \(P_{Bu}\) increases — H₂ and MEK are weakly adsorbed, so the deck's dual-site surface-reaction rate law is:

$$ r_{MEK}'=\frac{k\,P_{Bu}}{\left(1+K_{Bu}P_{Bu}\right)^{2}} $$

The squared denominator (two sites) is what creates the maximum, located at \(P_{Bu}=1/K_{Bu}\) with height \(k/4K_{Bu}\). The deck's regression gives \(k=0.4939,\ K_{Bu}=1.869\). Fit the six data points yourself:

Reading the hint ("some species might be weakly adsorbed"). H₂ and MEK adsorb weakly, so their K·P terms drop out of the denominator, leaving only KBuPBu. Deciding which terms to keep is the modelling — keep only what the data justify.
3.6

Catalyst deactivation: the rate that fades with time on stream

Real catalysts age; multiply the fresh rate by an activity a(t) that decays by its own law.

Sintering, poisoning and coking all steal active sites. Model it by scaling the fresh LHHW rate with a time-dependent activity \(a(t)\in[0,1]\), governed by a decay-rate law:

$$ -r_\mathrm{A}'(t)=a(t)\,\big[-r_{\mathrm{A},\,fresh}'\big]=a(t)\frac{k'C_\mathrm{A}C_\mathrm{B}}{1+K_\mathrm{A}C_\mathrm{A}+K_\mathrm{B}C_\mathrm{B}},\qquad -\frac{da}{dt}=k_d\,a^{\,p} $$

First-order decay (\(p=1\)) gives \(a=e^{-k_d t}\); second-order (\(p=2\), e.g. sintering) gives \(a=1/(1+k_d t)\). Run the reactor clock and watch activity — and the measured rate — fade:

Design consequence. To keep including deactivation, the decay-rate law is fitted alongside the kinetic law against time-resolved data — then the reactor is sized for the aged activity, not the fresh one. A catalyst that looks great on day one can still miss the spec by month three.
